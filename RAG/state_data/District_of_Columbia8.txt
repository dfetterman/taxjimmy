State: District of Columbia



Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients General Rule

Exempt Sales of food or drink that are considered “eligible foods”are not subject to tax. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2001(n)(2)(E). The exclusion for eligible foods applies regardless of whether the purchases are made with food stamps. The District of Columbia incorporates the definition of eligible foods in 7 C.F.R. §271.2. The following are exempt eligible foods:• any food or food product intended for human consumption except alcoholic beverages, tobacco, and hot foods and hot food products prepared for immediate consumption; • seeds and plants to grow foods for the personal consumption of eligible households; • meals prepared and delivered by an authorized meal delivery service to households eligible to use coupons to purchase delivered meals; or meals served by an authorized communal dining facility for the elderly, for SSI households or both, to households eligible to use coupons for communal dining; • meals prepared and served by a drug addict or alcoholic treatment and rehabilitation center to narcotic addicts or alcoholics and their children who live with them; • meals prepared and served by a group living arrangement facility to residents who are blind or disabled; • meals prepared by and served by a shelter for battered women and children to its eligible residents; • in the case of homeless food stamp households, meals prepared for and served by an authorized public or private nonprofit establishment (e.g., soup kitchen, temporary shelter), approved by an appropriate state or local agency, that feeds homeless persons; and • in the case of homeless food stamp households, meals prepared by a restaurant that contracts with an appropriate state agency to serve meals to homeless persons at concessional (low or reduced) prices. 7 C.F.R. §271.2; D.C. Code Ann. § 47-2001(n)(2)(E). Street Vendors Effective Oct. 1, 2012, street vendors in D.C. are no longer allowed to make a $375 quarterly payment in lieu of collecting and remitting sales tax on their sales, including sales of food, from public spaces or from door to door. Street vendors must collect tax on sales food items and remit the greater of a $375 quarterly payment, or the full amount of tax collected on the sales. D.C. Code § 47-2002.01, as amended by 2012 D.C. Act 19-355, effective July 13, 2012. Semipublic Institutions Food or drink described in §47-2001(n)(1)(A)that is delivered and sold without profit by a nonprofit volunteer organization to persons who are confined to their homes due to age, illness, disability, or infirmity are not subject to tax, so long as the organization has received a certificate of exemption from the District of Columbia as a semipublic institution. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2005(18); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2206(2). Meals and food products served by a semipublic personal or professional service institution or organization that holds a Certificate of Exemption to pupils, students, or patients are not subject to the sales tax whether or not prepared by the institutions. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, §443.1. Sales of tangible personal property by a semipublic institution, or any other entity, to employees, doctors, nurses, or the general public are considered retail sales subject to the sales tax. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, §443.3. Tangible personal property here includes meals, foods, or drink. Sales of tangible personal property, including meals and food products, on the premises of semipublic or other institutions are subject to the tax when made by any person other than a semipublic institution. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, §443.3. Other Institutions Meals and food products prepared and served by a convalescent home, educational institution, hospital, or similar institution that does not qualify as a semipublic institution to students, teachers, doctors, nurses, and employees are not subject to the sales tax, where no separate charge is made; however, if the non-exempt institution purchases food prepared by others, the purchases are subject to tax. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, §443.2. House of Representative Cloakrooms Sales of food or drink, beverages, and other goods made to any person for use in the operation of the majority and minority cloakrooms of the House of Representatives and sales of food or drink, beverages, and other goods made by any person in connection with the operation of the cloakrooms are not subject to tax. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2005(8); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2206(2). Meals Sold to Employees Meals or food furnished to employees or others are not subject to tax if the meals or foods are furnished instead of cash and recorded as part of the employee's compensation, or if the meals or foods are furnished to the employees or others for the employer's convenience. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, §443.5. Meals that are sold to employees or to the public in cafeterias, dining rooms, lunch counters, or other places that are operated for employees are subject to tax. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, §443.6. Trains, Aircraft, and Automobiles Sales of food or drink or beverages of any nature that are made in any car composing a part of any train or in any aircraft or boat operating within the District of Columbia in the course of commerce between the District of Columbia and a state are not subject to tax. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2005(9); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2206(2). BNA-SUTN DC 11.1.1.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Definition of 'Food and Food Ingredients'

Defined The term “food or drink” is used to describe items sold for human or animal ingestion that are consumed for their taste or nutritional value. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2001(g); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2201(j). These items include, but are not limited to, baby foods and formula; baked goods; baking soda, baking powder and baking mixes; bouillon; cereal and cereal products;cocoa and cocoa products; coffee and coffee substitutes; condiments;cooking wines; cough drops; edible cake decorations; egg and egg products;fish and fish products, including shellfish; fruit, fruit products, and fruit juices; gelatin; honey; ice cream; meat and meat products;milk and milk products; nondairy creamers; oleomargarine; pasta and pasta products; poultry and poultry products; powdered drinks, including health and diet drinks; salad dressings; salt and salt substitutes;sauces and gravies; snack foods; soups; spices and herbs; sugar and sugar products; syrup and syrup substitutes; tea and tea substitutes;vegetables, vegetable products, and vegetable juices; vitamins; water;yogurt; pet foods; flavored extracts; ice; and any combination of these items. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2001(g); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2201(j). The term “food or drink” does not include spirituous or malt liquors, beers and wines; drugs, medicines, or pharmaceuticals; chewing tobacco; toothpaste;or mouthwash. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2001(g); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2201(j). BNA-SUTN DC 11.1.2.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Definition of 'Prepared Food'

Defined The term “food or drink prepared for immediate consumption”includes food or drink sold in a heated state, except for heated baked goods, where the heated state is solely a result of the baking. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2001(g-1). The following items are also considered food or drink prepared for immediate consumption:• sandwiches suitable for immediate consumption; • prepared salads; • salad bars; • party platters; • cold drinks dispensed in or with a cup or glass either by a retailer or on a self-service basis by the consumer; • frozen yogurt, ice cream, or ice milk sold in quantities of less than one pint; and • all food or drink served by, or sold in, or sold by restaurants, lunch counters, cafeterias, hotels, caterers, boarding houses, carryout shops, or like places of business. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2001(g-1). BNA-SUTN DC 11.2.2.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Definition of 'Restaurant' or 'Eating Establishment'

Defined The District of Columbia defines the term “restaurant” as a retail establishment that is licensed by the District of Columbia, is a separately metered or submetered facility, and is in the principal business of preparing and serving food to the public. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2005(11A)(B). The term “restaurant”includes pizzerias, delicatessens, ice cream parlors, cafeterias, take-out counters, caterers, and banquet and food-processing areas in hotels, but does not include beverage counters, including coffee shops and juice bars. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2005(11A)(B). BNA-SUTN DC 11.2.3.

D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, §443.2. Additionally, food or drink described in §47-2001(n)(1)(A) that is delivered and sold without profit by a nonprofit volunteer organization to persons who are confined to their homes due to age, illness, disability, or infirmity are not subject to tax, so long as the organization has received a certificate of exemption from the District of Columbia as a semipublic institution. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2005(18); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2206(2). Senior Citizen Residences Sales of food or drink made by a residence for senior citizens to the residents, employees, and guests of the facilities are not subject to tax, provided that the facility does not make sales to the general public. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2005(19); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2206(2). A residence for senior citizens is any facility that rents or offers for rent rooms or dwelling units exclusively to persons who are 60 years of age or older or who are blind or have another disability. At least 80 percent of the residents of the facility must be 60 years of age or older. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2005(19); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2206(2). BNA-SUTN DC 11.2.8.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food Stamp and WIC Purchases

Exempt Purchases of “eligible foods” made with food stamps are not subject to tax. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2005(23); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2206(2); 7 C.F.R. §271.2. The term “eligible foods” includes meals prepared and delivered by an authorized meal delivery service to households eligible to use food stamps to purchase delivered meals, or meals served by an authorized communal dining facility for the elderly, for Supplemental Security Income households or both, to households eligible to use food stamps for communal dining. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, § 443.8. Additionally, the following items that would typically be subject to sales and use tax are tax-exempt when purchased with food stamps:• half-pint containers of milk; • candy bars(fewer than six); • canned or bottled sodas (fewer than six); • cold sandwiches;and • certain other foods packaged and purchased for immediate consumption. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, § 443.10. Food stamps cannot be used to purchase the following:• alcoholic beverages; • tobacco or cigarettes; • household supplies; • soaps; • paper products; • medicines or vitamins; • any other nonfood items; • food that will be eaten in the store; • hot foods that are ready to eat, such as barbecued chicken; and • pet foods. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, § 443.9. BNA-SUTN DC 11.4.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Catering and Personal Chefs

Taxable All sales of food by a caterer are sales of “food prepared for immediate consumption” and are subject to sales tax. D.C. Code Ann. §47-2001(g-1); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2001(n)(1)(A); D.C. Code Ann. §47-2201(a)(1)(E). Further, if a sales agreement with a caterer requires the caterer to prepare and serve food, meals, or beverages, and in addition provides that the caterer shall furnish waiters and other services connected with that preparation and service, the tax shall apply to the total charges, whether or not the charges for preparation and service and the charges for the waiters and other services are separately stated. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, § 436.2. Sales tax does not apply to charges for an announcer, hat check person, doorman, or other person who is not connected with the preparation or service of food, meals, or beverages, provided that charges for these services are separately stated. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, § 436.3. The term “caterer” means any person who is engaged in the business of preparing food, meals, or beverages for the purpose of consumption at a place designated by the purchaser. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, § 436.1. BNA-SUTN DC 11.5.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Mobile Food Vendors

Taxable Sales of food by street vendors are subject to sales tax. D.C. Code § 47-2002.01, as amended by 2012 D.C. Act 19-355, effective July 13, 2012. Prior to October 1, 2012, street vendors in D.C. were allowed to make a $375 quarterly payment in lieu of collecting and remitting sales tax on their sales, including sales of food, from public spaces or from door to door. As of October 1, 2012, street vendors must collect tax on sales food items and remit the greater of a $375 quarterly payment, or the full amount of tax collected on the sales. D.C. Code § 47-2002.01, as amended by 2012 D.C. Act 19-355, effective July 13, 2012. “Street vendors” are persons licensed to vend from a sidewalk, roadway, or other public space under D.C. Code § 47-2002.01. and the term “food or drink prepared for immediate consumption” includes sales made by a street or sidewalk vendor who has no fixed place of business, even though the vendor does not sell from a vehicle. D.C. Mun. Regs. tit. 9, §442.1. BNA-SUTN DC 11.7.
