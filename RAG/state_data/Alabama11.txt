State: Alabama



Topic: Imposition of Tax General Rule - Imposition of Tax Sales Tax

Yes The state of Alabama imposes a privilege or license tax at a rate of 4 percent on the gross proceeds of retail sales or rentals made by a business. Ala. Code §40-23-2(1). Additionally, tax is imposed at a rate of 4 percent on the gross receipts of any business engaged in operating places of amusement or entertainment. Ala. Code §40-23-2(2). Alabama does not impose tax on the sale of services performed within the state. Ala. Code §40-23-2(1). While the vendor bears the legal liability for the tax, the tax is added to the sales price and is collected by the retailer from the purchaser. Ala. Code §40-23-26(a). BNA-SUTN AL 1.1.1.

Topic: Imposition of Tax General Rule - Imposition of Tax Use Tax

Yes Alabama imposes an excise tax on the sales price of tangible personal property that is stored, used, or otherwise consumed within the state. Ala. Code §40-23-61. The general use tax rate for most tangible personal property is 4 percent. Ala. Code §40-23-61. Prior to April 6, 2018, use tax exemptions were listed in Ala. Code §40-23-62. Effective April 6, 2018, Ala. Code §40-23-62 incorporates the sales tax exemptions listed in Ala. Code §40-23-4 and Ala. Code §40-23-4.1 by reference. References to most subsections of Ala. Code §40-23-62 that enumerates an exemption are obsolete and have been removed. Ala. Code §40-23-62, as amended by 2018 Ala. H.B. 242 effective April 6, 2018. BNA-SUTN AL 1.1.2.

Topic: Imposition of Tax General Rule - Imposition of Tax Local Option Sales and Use Tax

Local Tax Authorized By ordinance, Alabama municipalities may impose a sales tax that parallels the state levy, except for the rate of tax imposed. Ala. Code §11-51-200; Ala. Code §11-51-202. Thus, taxes imposed by municipalities must conform to the state taxing authority on definitions, exceptions, exemptions, proceedings, requirements, provisions, and rules promulgated under the Alabama Administrative Procedure Act. Ala. Code §11-51-201(a). Municipalities may not impose taxes on the Alcoholic Beverage Control Board of the State of Alabama for the sale of alcoholic beverages. Ala. Code §11-51-200. A municipality is defined as any incorporated city or town in the state of Alabama. Ala. Code §11-99A-2(7). A council or other governing body has the authority to levy and assess sales and use taxes within the police jurisdiction of any municipality or town by ordinance and with a 30-day notice. The tax may not exceed half the amount levied for like businesses, sales or uses conducted within the corporate limits, with fees and penalties excluded. Ala. Code §11-51-206, as amended by 2015 Ala. H.B. 377 effective Sept. 1, 2015. The local sales and use tax rates range from 0.5 percent to 5 percent. Alabama Dept. of Rev., Local Tax Notices. Municipalities may also determine, by ordinance, the rate of sales and use tax within this range. Ala. Code §11-51-205(a). Additionally, local municipalities are subject to the same nexus and sourcing rules as the state sales and use taxes. Ala. Code §11-51-200; Ala. Code §11-51-201; Ala. Code §11-51-202. Additionally, counties may levy a local sales taxes that parallels the state levy, except for the rate of tax to be imposed. The proceeds of such taxes must be spent exclusively for educational purposes or, in one instance, in the construction of a jail. Ala. Code §40-12-4; 2017 Ala. H.B. 572 §8, effective May 11, 2017; Jefferson Cty., Ala. v. Taxpayers &Citizens of Jefferson Cty., 2017 BL 84275 (Ala. 2017) (state levy of sales and use tax in a specific county is constitutional and does not conflict with Ala. Code §40-12-4). The Alabama legislature may not pass or repeal any local law without notice to the affected county. Ala. Const. art. IV, §106; Ala. Const. art. IV, §107; Burnett v. Chilton Cnty. Health Care Auth. & Chilton Cnty., No. 1160958, 2018 BL 322534 (Ala. Aug. 31, 2018). BNA-SUTN AL 1.1.3.

Topic: Imposition of Tax General Rule - Imposition of Tax Excise and Replacement Taxes

Yes Excise Taxes Alabama imposes several excise taxes. Ala. Code §28-3-74; Ala. Code §28-3-200 through Ala. Code §28-3-207; Ala. Code §40-25-1 through Ala. Code §40-25-47; Ala. Code §27-4A-1 through Ala. Code §27-4A-7; Ala. Code §40-17-325. Those taxes include, but are not limited to:• an alcoholic beverage tax; Ala. Code §28-3-74; Ala. Code §28-3-200 through Ala. Code §28-3-207. • a cigarette tax; Ala. Code §40-25-1 through Ala. Code §40-25-47. • an insurance premium tax; Ala. Code §27-4A-1 through Ala. Replacement Taxes The Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement prohibits member states from imposing a “prohibited replacement tax” which is a tax outside of the sales and use tax that is imposed on products defined in the Agreement. Generally, the Streamlined Sales Tax Governing Board will make a finding regarding whether or not a member state has imposed a replacement tax. Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement, as amended through May 3, 2018, Section 334; Streamlined Sales Tax Governing Board Rules and Procedures, as amended through May 3, 2018, Rule 334. However, Alabama is not a member of the Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement. Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Governing Board, State Info. BNA-SUTN AL 1.1.4.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Historical Information

Est. 1939 Alabama first imposed a sales tax at the rate of 2 percent for general sales and 0.5 percent for automobile sales in 1939. A Legislator's Guide to Alabama's Taxes. The rate rose to 3 percent in 1959 for general sales and 1.5 percent for automobiles. The statewide rate has since risen to 4 percent for general sales and 2 percent for automobiles. Unlike the corporate income and property tax rates in Alabama, the sales tax rate has no constitutional limitation. A Legislator's Guide to Alabama's Taxes. Prior to April 6, 2018, use tax exemptions were listed in Ala. Code §40-23-62. Effective April 6, 2018, there was a change in the statutory structure governing sales and use tax exemptions. Specifically, 2018 Ala. H.B. 242, incorporates the sales tax exemptions listed in Ala. Code §40-23-4 and Ala. Code §40-23-4.1 by reference into Ala. Code §40-23-62. Most references to a subsection of Ala. Code §40-23-62 that enumerates an exemption are obsolete and have been removed. Ala. Code §40-23-62, as amended by 2018 Ala. H.B. 242 effective April 6, 2018. BNA-SUTN AL 1.2.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Pending and Trending Developments

Nexus Effective Jan. 1, 2016, Alabama expanded its sales tax jurisdiction to digital sellers with no in-state physical presence. Ala. Admin. Code r. 810-6-2-.90.03. By regulation, Alabama provides that even if a seller lacks a physical presence in Alabama, the seller must collect and remit sales tax if the seller conducts one or more of the activities described in Ala. Code §40-23-68 and the seller's retail sales of tangible personal property sold into Alabama exceed $250,000 per year based on the previous calendar year's sales. Ala. Admin. Code r. 810-6-2-.90.03; see also Alabama Dept. of Rev., Frequently Asked Questions - Are Sales of Goods Made Via the Internet Taxable? Alabama intended for Ala. Admin. Code r. 810-6-2-.90.03 to be a direct challenge to the physical presence requirement promulgated by the U.S. Supreme Court in Quill Corp. v. North Dakota, 504 U.S. 298 (1992), overruled in part by South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc., 138 S. Ct. 2080, 2018 BL 219995 (U.S. 2018). Newegg, Inc. v. Ala., Dept. of Rev., Ala. Tax Trib., No. S. 16-613, appeal filed June 8, 2016 Notice of Appeal. On July 3, 2018, the Alabama Department of Revenue issued guidance for online sellers stating that the Department will apply Ala. Admin. Code r. 810-6-2-.90.03 prospectively for sales made on or after Oct. 1, 2018. Alabama Dept. of Rev., ADOR Announces Sales and Use Tax Guidance for Online Sellers (July 3, 2018). Newegg, Inc. v. Alabama Department of Revenue On June 8, 2016, Newegg, Inc., filed the first appeal challenging Alabama's regulation. Newegg, Inc. v. Ala., Dept. of Rev., Ala. Tax Trib., No. S. 16-613, appeal filed June 8, 2016 Notice of Appeal. On May 11, 2018, the Alabama Tax Tribunal denied the department's motion to hold the proceedings in abeyance pending the U.S. Supreme Court's decision in South Dakota v. Wayfair. Alabama Administrative Law Judge Opinion and Preliminary Order (May 11, 2018). On June 14, 2018, the Alabama Tax Tribunal voided the assessment against Newegg. The Alabama Department of Revenue had agreed that the tribunal should void the assessment unless and until the Quill Corp. v. North Dakota standard is revised. On June 21, 2018, the Supreme Court released an opinion in South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc. overruling the physical presence standards of Quill Corp. v. North Dakota and Natl. Bellas Hess, Inc. v. Illinois Dept. of Rev. South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc., 138 S. Ct. 2080, 2018 BL 219995 (U.S. 2018) overruling in part Quill Corp. v. North Dakota, 504 U.S. 298 (1992), and Natl. Bellas Hess, Inc. v. Illinois Dept. of Rev., 386 U.S. 753 (1967); Alabama Administrative Law Judge Ruling Docket No. S. 16-613-JP (June 14, 2018)(voiding the assessment against Newegg). BNA-SUTN AL 1.3.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Key Definitions 'Sale' and 'Retail Sale'

Defined Sale In Alabama, a “sale” includes installment and credit sales, all exchanges pursuant to which title or ownership passes, all sales of property for money, and every closed transaction constituting a sale. Ala. Code §40-23-1(a)(5); Ala. Admin. Code r. 810-6-1-.150. However, the definition does not include transfers of property by gift. Ala. Code §40-23-1(a)(5); Ala. Admin. Code r. 810-6-1-.150. If a lease or rental agreement contemplates or has the ultimate purpose of transferring ownership of the leased property when a certain number of payments or other requirements are met, then the transaction will be considered a sale, not a lease. Ala. Admin. Code r. 810-6-1-.89. Otherwise, lease or rental agreements are not considered sales, but are subject to a separate privilege or license tax in Alabama. Ala. Code §40-12-222. All LLCs, including both single-member and multiple-member LLCs organized on or after Jan. 1, 1997, will be classified as they are classified for federal income tax purposes under the Internal Revenue Service's ‘check-the-box’ regulations. Ala. Code §40-18-28; Alabama Revenue Ruling 98-005 (June 18, 1998). Accordingly, a domestic organization with only one member that is owned by a business is disregarded as a separate organization for tax purposes, such that sales between the two entities are disregarded and not subject to sales tax. Ala. Code §40-18-28; Alabama Revenue Ruling 98-005 (June 18, 1998). Retail Sale A “retail sale”is defined as any sale of tangible personal property except sales made at wholesale. Ala. Code §40-23-1(a)(10); Ala. Code §40-23-1(a)(9). A “retail sale”in Alabama means all sales of tangible personal property, except for wholesale sales, including sales of:• building materials to contractors, builders, or landowners for resale or use in the form of real estate; • building materials, fixtures, or other equipment to a manufacturer or builder of modular buildings for use in manufacturing, building, or equipping a modular building ultimately to become a part of real estate in Alabama; • tangible personal property to undertakers and morticians; or • tangible personal property or products to manufacturers, quarry operators, mine operators, or compounders that are used or consumed by them in manufacturing, mining, quarrying, or compounding and do not become an ingredient or component part of the tangible personal property manufactured or compounded. Ala. Code §40-23-1(a)(10). The term “sale at retail” or “retail sale” also includes the withdrawal, use, or consumption of any tangible personal property by anyone who purchased that tangible personal property at wholesale. Ala. Code §40-23-1(a)(10); Alabama Administrative Law Judge Ruling Docket No. S. 04-917 (Nov. 8, 2005); Alabama Revenue Ruling 01-010 (Aug. 15, 2001). Sale at Wholesale A sale at wholesale includes any of the following transactions:• sales of tangible personal property by wholesalers to licensed retailers, jobbers, dealers, or other wholesalers for resale; • sales of tangible personal property or products including iron ore, and including furnished containers and labels of such property, to a manufacturer or compounder that enter into and become an ingredient or component part of a finished product manufactured or compounded for sale; • sales of non-returnable containers to vendors who sell or give patrons the containers in conjunction with customer purchases; • sales of pallets intended for one-time use; • sales of crowns, caps, and tops intended for one-time use employed and used on the containers in which a manufacturer or compounder markets the products; • sales of containers to vendors engaged in selling, supplying, or furnishing baby chicks to growers that use the containers for delivery of baby chicks, and eggs, even if the containers are reused; • sales of bagging and ties used in preparing cotton for market; • sales to meat packers, manufacturers, compounders, or processors of meat products of all casings used to mold or form wieners and Vienna sausages even though casings may be reused; • sales of commercial fish feed, including concentrates, supplements, and other feed ingredients when these substances are used in mixing and preparing feed for fish that are raised for the purpose of sale on a commercial basis; • sales of tangible personal property to any person engaging in the business of leasing or renting tangible personal property to others, if the property is purchased for the purpose of leasing or renting to others, and the property is taxed under the rental and lease privilege and licensing tax; or • purchases or withdrawals of parts or materials from stock, when the parts are used in repairing or reconditioning the tangible personal property. Ala. Code §40-23-1(a)(9)(a) through Ala. Code §40-23-1(a)(9)(k). BNA-SUTN AL 1.4.1.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Key Definitions 'Lease'

Defined In Alabama, “leasing” has several meanings including a transaction where a person who owns or controls tangible personal property permits another person to possess or use the property for consideration and without a transfer of title. Ala. Code §40-12-220. “Leasing” is fully defined as a transaction whereunder the person who owns or controls the possession of tangible personal property permits another person to have the possession or use thereof for a consideration and for the duration of a definite or indefinite period of time without transfer of the title to such property. Ala. Code §40-12-220. BNA-SUTN AL 1.4.2.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Key Definitions 'Use' or 'Storage, Use, or Consumption'

Defined In Alabama, “use” is the exercise of any right or power over tangible personal property incident to the ownership of the property, or by any transaction giving possession. Ala. Code §40-23-60(8). Alabama defines “storage”as any keeping or retention in Alabama for any purpose, except subsequent use solely outside Alabama. Ala. Code §40-23-60(7). However, “use”and “storage” do not include the sale of property in the regular course of business. Ala. Code §40-23-60(8); Alabama Administrative Law Judge Ruling Docket No. S. 11-541 (Nov. 22, 2011) (because taxpayer purchased boat from a person not licensed as an Alabama retailer, and the boat was then used and stored in Alabama, the boat is subject to use tax); Alabama Administrative Law Judge Ruling Docket No. S. 04-203 (April 18, 2005) (truck tractors and trailers brought in from out-of-state and used in Alabama are subject to use tax even though the property was first used elsewhere in interstate commerce, as taxpayer intended to use the tractors in all of the states in which it operates, including Alabama). BNA-SUTN AL 1.4.3.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Key Definitions 'Retailer' or 'Vendor'

Not Defined Alabama does not provide a definition for the term “retailer.” Ala. Code §40-23-1. However, the state imposes sales tax on the gross proceeds of retail sales against the “person”on account of a business. “Person” or “company,”used interchangeably in Alabama, includes any individual, firm, copartnership, association, corporation, receiver, trustee, or any other group or combination acting as a unit. Ala. Code §40-23-1(a)(1). BNA-SUTN AL 1.4.6.
