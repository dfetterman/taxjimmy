State: Connecticut



Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients General Rule

Exempt Connecticut exempts from sales and use tax sales of food products for human consumption. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(13). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.1.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Definition of 'Food and Food Ingredients'

Defined Connecticut defines “food products for human consumption” as all products normally consumed by human beings for nutritive value or taste satisfaction. Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(b); Connecticut Ruling 2016-2 (April 29, 2016). “Food products”include:• cereals and cereal products; • milk and milk products; • oleomargarine; • meat and meat products; • fish and fish products; • eggs and egg products; • vegetables and vegetable products; Connecticut Ruling 2016-3 (Sept.15, 2016) (the aloe plant, when raised for edible products, can be considered a vegetable). • fruit and fruit products; • spices and salt; • sugar and sugar products other than candy and confectionery; • coffee and coffee substitutes; • tea; and • cocoa and cocoa products other than candy and confectionery. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(13). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.2.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Grocery Items

Exempt Food products for human consumption are tax-exempt in Connecticut. Connecticut does not address food products using the term “grocery products.” Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(13); see also Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2002(2). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.1.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Candy

Taxable Connecticut imposes sales and use tax on sales of candy. This is because the term “food products” excludes candy and confectionery, including candied fruit, candy coated popcorn, chewing gum, breath mints, and carob. Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(b)(2). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.2.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Dietary Supplements and Vitamins

Exempt Chewable tablets and capsules made from fruits, vegetables and grains that are marketed as natural nutrients to be consumed for nutritive value are considered food products rather than dietary supplements. Connecticut Ruling 2016-2 (April 29, 2016). Vitamins purchased on prescription are exempt from Connecticut sales and use tax. However, when such drugs or medicine are purchased by a doctor, dentist, or veterinarian for use in their profession, the drugs or medicine are exempt from sales and use tax regardless of whether they are purchased with a prescription. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(48); Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-13. Connecticut repealed the exemption for non-prescription medicines, effective from July 1, 2011, to March 31, 2015. 2011 Conn. H.B. 6652, as enacted by 2011 Conn. S.B. 1239. However, the exemption for non-prescription medicines resumed beginning April 1, 2015. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(48), as added by 2014 Conn. H.B. 5596, effective July 1, 2014. Ready-to-drink beverages and liquid or powdered concentrate made from aloe, marketed for use as a beverage, and labeled with the “Nutrition Facts”box, are also considered food products for human consumption, not dietary supplements. Connecticut Ruling 2016-3 (Sept. 15, 2016). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.3.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Alcoholic Beverages

Taxable Connecticut imposes sales and use tax on sales of alcoholic beverages. This is because the term “food products” excludes alcoholic beverages and mixes (except for wines made for use in cooking). Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(b)(1). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.4.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Non-Alcoholic Beverages

Depends Carbonated beverages are taxable; however non-carbonated beverages that do not contain alcohol are exempt food products. Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(b)(3); see Connecticut Ruling 2015-4 (Sept. 16, 2015). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.5.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Carbonated Beverages

Taxable Connecticut imposes sales and use tax on sales of carbonated beverages. This is because the term “food products” excludes carbonated beverages, including carbonated water. Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(b)(3); see Connecticut Ruling 2015-4 (Sept. 16, 2015). For information on excise taxes on carbonated beverages in Connecticut, see the Excise Tax Navigator, at Connecticut 14.1. BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.6.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Non-Carbonated Beverages

Exempt Connecticut does not limit the exemption for food products with respect to “noncarbonated beverages.” Therefore, sales of noncarbonated beverages are exempt from sales and use tax. Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(b)(3). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.7.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Juice

Exempt Connecticut exempts sales of fruit juice from sales and use tax. This is because food products for human consumption are not subject to tax in Connecticut, and “food products” includes “fruit and fruit products.” Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(13). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.8.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Soft Drinks

Taxable Carbonated soft drinks are subject to sales tax; however, non-carbonated beverages that do not contain alcohol are exempt. Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(b)(3); see Connecticut Ruling 2015-4 (Sept. 16, 2015). For information on excise taxes on soft drinks in Connecticut, see the Excise Tax Navigator, at Connecticut 14.1. BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.9.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Bottled Water

Depends Carbonated water is excluded from the definition of tax-exempt “food products.” Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(b)(3); Connecticut Ruling 2015-4 (Sept. 16, 2015). Further, bottles of water sold in containers of 16 ounces or less are taxable as food for immediate consumption. Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2002(2) (Feb. 22, 2002). However, non-carbonated bottled water sold in multi-packs is tax-exempt. Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2002(2) (Feb. 22, 2002). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.10.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Water, Other Than Bottled Water

Exempt Water delivered through mains, lines, pipes, or bottles is tax-exempt. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(3)(C). BNA-SUTN CT 11.1.3.11.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Other Food Products

Taxable Enumerated Taxable Foods Connecticut enumerates several types of purchases that do not qualify for the food exemption and are therefore taxable. Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(b)(2); Conn. Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food General Rule

Taxable Meals sold by eating establishments or caterers are subject to sales tax. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(13); Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(c); Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2002(2). The following types of sales are not subject to tax:• sales of meals made at a school or college to students or staff thereof; Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(d)(1). • sales of meals to patients at a hospital, home for the aged, convalescent home, nursing home, or rest home; Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(d)(2). • sales of meals made to a charitable or religious organization that holds a sales tax exemption or to an agency of the U.S., the state of Connecticut, or a Connecticut municipality, provided all the following conditions are met: o the seller directly charges the organization or governmental agency for the meals; Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(d)(3)(A). o the organization or governmental agency directly pays (by check on its own account) the seller; Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(d)(3)(B). o the organization or governmental agency is not reimbursed, directly or indirectly, for its payment for the meals by those who consumed the meals; Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(d)(3)(C). • sales of meals made by a nonprofit organization to an elderly, disabled, or other homebound person if the meal is delivered to the home of such person; Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(d)(4). and • occasional sales of meals by a nonprofit organization as described in Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-17. Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(d)(5). Furthermore, no tax is due from an employer who serves meals on his premises to his employees at no separate charge or deduction. Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(e). For more information on meals taxes in Connecticut, see the Excise Tax Navigator, at Connecticut 10.2. BNA-SUTN CT 11.2.1.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Definition of 'Prepared Food'

Defined “Meals”are food products for human consumption sold in such form and such portions that they are ready for immediate consumption and include items sold for take-out. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(13); Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(c); Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2002(2). Meals do not include bulk sales of food products unless meant for consumption at or near the seller's location. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(13); Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(c); Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2002(2). Examples of such bulk sales include the sale of ice cream in one-half gallon containers, whole pies, cold sliced meat sold by the pound and cold salads sold by the pound, but do not include the sale of whole pizza pies and buckets of fried chicken. Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(c). BNA-SUTN CT 11.2.2.

==================================================

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Definition of 'Restaurant' or 'Eating Establishment'

Defined “Eating establishment” means a place where meals are sold. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(13); Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(c); Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2002(2). “Eating establishment”specifically includes a restaurant, cafeteria, grinder shop, pizzeria, drive-in, fast food outlet, ice cream truck, hot dog cart, refreshment stand, sandwich shop, private or social club, cocktail lounge, tavern, diner, snack bar, or hotel or boarding house that furnishes both lodging and meals to its guests. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(13); Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(c); Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2002(2). BNA-SUTN CT 11.2.3.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Prepared Food Items Served at Nursing Homes and Other Long-term Care Facilities

Exempt Nonprofit charitable hospitals, nursing homes, rest homes, residential care homes, and sole community hospitals are exempt from sales or use tax on certain sales of prepared food items. Conn. Gen. Gen. Additionally, meals and food items served or sold on the premises to patients in hospitals, or residents or care recipients of residential care homes, convalescent homes, nursing homes, rest homes, assisted living facilities, senior centers, and day care centers are exempt from sales tax. Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-412(9) BNA-SUTN CT 11.2.8.

Taxable Connecticut imposes sales and use tax on meals provided by catering services. Conn. Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Complimentary Meals

Nontaxable Complimentary meals are not subject to tax because Connecticut's sales and use tax and room occupancy tax only apply where there are sales for consideration. Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2003(1). However, barter transactions are treated as sales for consideration. This means that “complimentary”meals provided to hotel or restaurant employees can be subject to tax if provided as part of the employee's compensation. See Conn. Agencies Regs. §12-426-29(e); Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2001(13). BNA-SUTN CT 11.6.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Restaurant Equipment

Taxable Restaurant equipment is subject to sales and use tax because caterers and eating establishments are the final consumer of items that they purchase or lease for use in furnishing meals. See Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-408; Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-411; Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2002(2). Examples of items that sellers of meals use or consume in providing meals include linens, tablecloths, chairs, tables, china, silverware, food warmers, chafing dishes, tents, and cooking equipment. See Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-408; Conn. Gen. Stat. §12-411; Connecticut Policy Statement PS 2002(2). BNA-SUTN CT 11.8.

Depends Sales of exempt food through vending machines are tax-exempt. Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Other Food-Related Transactions

Taxable Where a delivery company purchases meals from a restaurant and resells and delivers the meals to customers for a higher price, plus a delivery fee, the delivery company must collect sales tax on the meals that it sells to its customers and include the delivery fee in its gross receipts. Connecticut Ruling 2018-1 (May 15, 2018). The delivery company can provide a resale certificate to the restaurant to purchase the meals without paying sales tax. Connecticut Ruling 2018-1 (May 15, 2018). BNA-SUTN CT 11.10.
