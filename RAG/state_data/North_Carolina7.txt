North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-2.I. North Carolina exempts from sales and use tax food provided to a person under a prepaid meal plan. Effective July 11, 2016, North Carolina also exempts related packaging items that are used to package, ship, or deliver the food; constitute a part of the sale; and are delivered with the food. A retailer that contracts with a food service contractor to provide food or prepared food under a prepaid meal plan, may include in the agreement that the food service contractor is liable for reporting and remitting sales tax due on the gross receipts derived from the prepaid meal plan on behalf of the retailer. The tax applies to the allocated sales price of the prepaid meal plan paid by or on behalf of the person entitled to use the meal plan, not to the amount charged by the food service contractor to the retailer under the agreement. A retailer electing this option must report to the food service contractor, the gross receipts a person pays to the retailer for a prepaid meal plan. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.16A. Sales of prepared food or meals by railroads, Pullman cars, steamships, airlines or other transportation company diners, while within North Carolina, are subject to the general rate of state tax and any applicable local sales or use tax. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-6. A summer camp that makes a weekly or monthly meal charge to persons who are enrolled in the course or activities carried on by the summer camp are not liable for collecting tax on the charges. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-5. Sales of food to summer camps for use in providing meals to campers as part of the operation of the camp and its programs are subject only to the local sales or use tax except that items such as prepared foods, soft drinks, and candy are subject to the combined state and local tax. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-5. Camps that operate cafeterias or restaurants where they make sales of meals and other tangible personal property to students or other users or consumers must register and collect and remit the general rate of state tax and any applicable local sales or use tax on the sales price of the property. Camps where rooms, lodging or accommodations are regularly furnished to transients for a consideration are deemed to be retailers and must collect and remit tax on the receipts. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-5. Sales of food and prepared food sold within the school building during the regular school day are exempt from sales and use tax in North Carolina. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(26) as amended by 2013 N.C. H.B. 998, effective Jan. 1, 2014;North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-1.B.4. For purposes of this exemption, the term “school”is an entity regulated under Chapter 115C of the General Statutes. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(26b), enacted by 2016 N.C. S.B. 729, §3.9(b), effective Jan. 1, 2017. For purposes of this exemption, the term “school” is an entity regulated under Chapter 115C of the General Statutes. Sales of meals not for profit to elderly and incapacitated persons by charitable or religious organizations not operated for profit that are entitled to the refunds provided by N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.14(b) are exempt from sales and use tax when such meals are delivered to the purchasers at their places of abode. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(31); North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-1.B.7. Sales of food by a church or religious organization not operated for profit when the sales are actually used for religious activities are exempt from sales and use tax in North Carolina. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(31a); North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-1.B.8. Sales of food products to registered merchants for resale, including sales to registered restaurants if the sales are supported by a properly executed North Carolina Form E-595E: Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement Certificate of Exemption are exempt from sales and use tax. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-1.B.1. Fraternities and sororities are considered student organizations, and when they operate dining rooms that serve meals or foodstuffs to students at educational institutions, the meals and food products are exempt from sales tax. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-4. Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Definition of 'Restaurant' or 'Eating Establishment'

Defined North Carolina defines “restaurant” as an establishment substantially engaged in the business of preparing and serving meals. To qualify as a restaurant, an establishment's gross receipts from food and nonalcoholic beverages cannot be less than thirty percent (30%) of the total gross receipts from food, nonalcoholic beverages, and alcoholic beverages. N.C. Gen. Stat. §18B-1000(6). A restaurant must also have a kitchen and an inside dining area with seating for at least 36 people. N.C. Gen. Stat. §18B-1000(6). North Carolina defines “eating establishment” as an establishment engaged in the business of regularly and customarily selling food, primarily to be eaten on the premises. Eating establishments shall include businesses that are referred to as restaurants, cafeterias, or cafes, but that do not qualify satisfy the definition of restaurant under N.C. Gen. Stat. §18B-1000(6). Eating establishments also include lunch stands, grills, snack bars, fast-food businesses, and other establishments, such as drugstores, which have a lunch counter or other section where food is sold to be eaten on the premises. N.C. Gen. Stat. §18B-1000(2). BNA-SUTN NC 11.2.3.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Prepared Food Items Served at Nursing Homes and Other Long-term Care Facilities

Depends Whether purchases of food items served at nursing homes and other long-term care facilities is taxable depends on whether the food could be purchased under the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program. N.C. Admin. Code tit. 17, r. 7B.1801;. Purchases of food by hospitals, sanitariums, nursing homes, or rest homes for use in furnishing meals to patients are exempt from State tax, but not the 2% local tax, if the food could be purchased under the Food Stamp Program. If food purchased by an institution could not be purchased under that Program, the food is subject to both State and local sales or use tax. N.C. Admin. Code tit. 17, r. 7B.1801. If, in addition to furnishing meals to patients, one of these institutions operates a cafeteria from which it makes sales of prepared meals or food to guests, visitors, employees, staff, or other persons, the institution must register with the Department of Revenue and collect and remit the tax on its sales. If the food purchased by the institution for use in furnishing meals to patients cannot be distinguished from the food purchased for resale through the cafeteria, the institution may purchase all the food under a certificate of resale. An institution that does this assumes liability for payment of sales or use tax on food used in furnishing meals to its patients and on sales of meals by the cafeteria. N.C. Admin. Code tit. 17, r. 7B.1801. BNA-SUTN NC 11.2.8.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Prepared Food Items Served at Educational Institutions

Depends Certain prepared food items served at educational institutions are exempt from tax. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(26). N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(26b). BNA-SUTN NC 11.2.9.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food Stamp and WIC Purchases

Exempt Food and other items lawfully purchased with debit cards issued under the Food Stamp Program, supplemental foods lawfully purchased with a food instrument issued under the Special Supplemental Food Program and supplemental foods for direct distribution by the Special Supplemental Food Program, are exempt from tax. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(38), as amended by 2019 N.C. S. 56, §5, effective March 20, 2019; North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-1.B.10. Under the Food Stamp Program, food means any food or food product for human consumption, and also includes seeds and plants for use in home gardens to produce food for consumption by food stamp households. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(38); North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-1.B.10. The supplemental foods list currently includes whole, skim, evaporated and nonfat milk, specific cereals, certain juices, cheese, eggs, dry beans and peas, dry infant cereal, infant juice, and other special formulas. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(38); North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-1.B.10. Items that may not be purchased with a debit card generally include alcoholic beverages, tobacco, hot foods ready to eat, and foods intended to be heated in the store, lunch counter items or foods to be eaten in a store, vitamins or medicines, pet foods, and nonfood items. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(38); North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-1.B.10. However, effective September 16, 2018, through October 31, 2018, hot foods and foods ready for immediate consumption are eligible for purchase with SNAP EBT benefits in North Carolina. If purchased with SNAP EBT benefits, these items are exempt from sales and use tax. North Carolina Important Notice: Disaster Assistance Sales and Use Tax Exemptions (Sept. 20, 2018). BNA-SUTN NC 11.4.

Taxable All charges by persons engaged in the catering business that are connected with furnishing, preparing, or serving of meals, foods, and other tangible personal property to users or consumers are subject to the general rate of state tax and any applicable local sales or use tax. Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Complimentary Meals

Depends If the retailer does not operate a restaurant and provides complimentary meals to employees or other patrons, the purchase of the food items used would be subject to local sales and use tax. However, restaurants may offer employees or patrons complimentary meals not subject to sales and use tax. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.12C; North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-11B. When property is given away or used by any retailer or wholesale merchant, the property given away is not considered sold, whether or not the retailer or wholesale merchant recovers the cost of the property from sales of other property. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.12C; North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-11B. When a hotel, motel, bed and breakfast inn, or similar business provides a complimentary meal or snack without charge and does not operate a restaurant or similar place in which it sells prepared food, qualifying food is subject only to the local tax, but prepared food, soft drinks, and candy are subject to the combined state and local tax at the time of the purchase. The food that is used by the business rather than resold is an operating expense similar to other expenses subject to sales or use tax such as linens, toiletries, and cleaning supplies. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-10.A. If, in addition to providing complimentary food, a hotel, motel, or bed and breakfast inn operates a restaurant or similar place that sells prepared food, the food used in providing the complimentary meal or snack is considered sold along with prepared food and is not subject to sales or use tax. The business should purchase all food for resale without paying tax on the food by completing a Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement Certificate of Exemption, Form E-585E. The business is liable for collecting and remitting the general rate of state tax and any applicable local sales tax on the retail sales price of the prepared meals or snacks when sold. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-10.B. Restaurants are not liable for use tax on the cost of meals furnished to employees, food served to bar patrons, and matches, when these items are furnished without charge pursuant to a decision by the North Carolina Court of Appeals. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-11.A; see In re Rock-ola Café, 111 N.C. App. 683 (1993). The taxpayer in the case was operating restaurants and made retail sales of beverages, sandwiches, and meals. The restaurants issued Certificate of Resale, Form E-590, to its suppliers and purchased foodstuffs without payment of sales taxes. The restaurants utilized the foodstuffs, in part, to provide snacks, sandwiches, and hor d'oeurves (bar food) to bar patrons, and meals to employees. The restaurants did not charge patrons for bar food and provided meals to employees without charge. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-11.A; see In re Rock-ola Café, 111 N.C. App. 683 (1993). BNA-SUTN NC 11.6.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Vending Machines

Taxable Sales of food through vending machines are subject to state and local sales and use tax in North Carolina. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(50); Streamlined Sales Tax Governing Board, Taxability Matrix, North Carolina (version 2018.0); N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13B(a)(3). Food sold through vending machines, which is regarded as food that is dispensed from a machine or another mechanical device that accepts payment, is subject to tax. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.3(11); N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(50); Streamlined Sales Tax Governing Board, Taxability Matrix, North Carolina (version 2018.0). Any person who makes a sale of tangible personal property through vending machines is required to register with the North Carolina Department of Revenue and pay sales tax on the sales price of tangible personal property in excess of one cent per sale. One cent sales through vending machines are exempt from sales tax when made by the owner or lessee of the vending machines. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(30); N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(50); N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13B; North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-9.A. Otherwise, food sold through vending machines is taxed on 50 percent of the sales price. North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-9.A. Vending machine operators are permitted to separate their receipts that are 100 percent taxable from the receipts that are 50 percent taxable and, after calculating the taxable amounts of each, may divide those total taxable amounts by 100 percent plus the combined percentage of the state and local rates of tax to arrive at a taxable sales reportable on their sales and use tax return. N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13(50); N.C. Gen. Stat. §105-164.13B; North Carolina Sales and Use Tax Bulletin Section 19-9.A. BNA-SUTN NC 11.9.
