State: Utah



Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients General Rule

Taxable Utah incorporates the definition of food and food ingredients as found in the Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(51)(a), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019. Sales of food and food ingredients in Utah are subject to a reduced state tax rate of 1.75 percent, with certain exceptions. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(2)(c). Food Purchased with Food Stamps Utah exempts from sales tax the purchase of any food using coupons issued under the federal Food Stamp Program. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-104(21); Utah Publication No. 25. BNA-SUTN UT 11.1.1.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Definition of 'Food and Food Ingredients'

Defined Food and food ingredients are substances, whether in liquid, concentrated, solid, frozen, dried, or dehydrated form, sold for ingestion or chewing by humans and are consumed for taste or nutritional value. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(1); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(51), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019; Utah Publication No. 25. “Grocery food”as used in Utah Tax Commission forms and publications means “food and food ingredients.” Utah Publication No. 25. BNA-SUTN UT 11.1.2.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Grocery Items

Taxable Sales of grocery items in Utah are subject to a reduced state tax rate of 1.75 percent as “food and food ingredients.” Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(2)(c); Utah Publication No. 25 (“grocery food” as used in Utah Tax Commission forms and publications means “food and food ingredients”); see also Utah Tax Comn., Rates Applied to Certain Transactions, (Feb. 10, 2017). “Food and food ingredients”does not include alcoholic beverages, tobacco, or prepared food. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(51), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019; Utah Publication No. 25. BNA-SUTN UT 11.1.3.1.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Candy

Taxable As an item sold for ingestion or chewing by humans, the sale of candy most likely is taxed at a reduced rate applied to food and food ingredients. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(1); Streamlined Sales Tax Governing Board, Taxability Matrix, Utah (version 2018.0); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(51), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019. “Candy” is defined as a preparation of sugar, honey, or other natural or artificial sweeteners in combination with chocolate, fruits, nuts or other ingredients or flavorings in the form of bars, drops, or pieces that require no refrigeration, but does not include preparations containing flour. Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement, as amended through Dec. 14, 2018, Appendix C, Part II. BNA-SUTN UT 11.1.3.2.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Dietary Supplements and Vitamins

Taxable Dietary supplements are tangible personal property, and their sales are subject to sales and use tax. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(1); Streamlined Sales Tax Governing Board, Taxability Matrix, Utah (version 2018.0). However, dietary supplements are considered food, and their sales are taxed at a reduced rate if nutritional fact boxes or supplemental fact boxes are provided on the labels. A drug fact box on the label means the item is not food. See Utah Private Letter Ruling No. 08-009 (Oct. 9, 2008) (over-the-counter dietary supplements could not be excluded from food and food ingredients unless they fell within categories of (1) alcoholic beverages, (2) tobacco, (3) prepared food, or (4)drugs); see also Utah Publication No. 25 (dietary supplements are not considered drugs); Utah State Tax Comn., Sales and Use Tax Rate on Grocery Food. “Dietary supplement” is defined as any product, other than tobacco, intended to supplement the diet that:• contains either a vitamin, mineral, herb or other botanical, an amino acid, a dietary substance that increases the dietary intake, or a concentrate, metabolite, constituent, extract, or combination of any of the foregoing ingredients; • is intended for ingestion in tablet, capsule, powder, softgel, gelcap, or liquid form and is not represented conventional food or a meal;and • is required to be labeled as a dietary supplement, identifiable by the “Supplemental Facts”box found on the label and as required pursuant to 21 C.F.R. §101.36. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(35), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019; Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement, as amended through Dec. 14, 2018, Appendix C, Part II. BNA-SUTN UT 11.1.3.3.

Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(10), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019; Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement, as amended through Dec. 14, 2018, Appendix C, Part II. Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Bottled Water

Taxable In Utah, retail sales of bottled water are taxable sales of tangible personal property. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(1); Streamlined Sales Tax Governing Board, Taxability Matrix, Utah (version 2018.0). Bottled water, including flavored water, is considered food and its sale is taxed at the reduced rate applied to sales of food and food ingredients. Utah State Tax Comn., Sales and Use Tax Rate on Grocery Food. Under the Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement, “bottled water” means water that is placed in a safety sealed container or package for human consumption, calorie-free and does not contain sweeteners or other additives. Bottled water includes water delivered to the purchaser in a reusable container that is not sold with the water. Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement, as amended through Dec. 14, 2018, Appendix C, Part II. BNA-SUTN UT 11.1.3.10.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Coffee

Depends Whether coffee is taxable depends on whether it is sold for immediate consumption. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(1); Streamlined Sales Tax Governing Board, Taxability Matrix, Utah (version 2018.0); Utah State Tax Comn., Sales and Use Tax Rate on Grocery Food. Accordingly, coffee that is sold with a straw, or in a to-go cup, is considered taxable at the regular rate, where coffee sold in a sealed-bottle form is subject to tax at the reduced rate. Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food General Rule

Taxable In Utah, “food and food ingredients” does not include prepared food. Thus, sales of prepared foods do not qualify for the reduced tax rate applied to sales of food and food ingredients, and are taxed at the general tax rate. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(2)(c); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(1)(e); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(51)(c)(iii), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019. Sales of the ingredients that become a component part of taxable meals are regarded as sales for resale exempt from tax. Utah Admin. Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Definition of 'Prepared Food'

Defined “Prepared food” is food sold in a heated state or heated by the seller, or two or more food ingredients mixed or combined by the seller for sale as a single item. “Prepared food” is also any food sold with an eating utensil provided by the seller, excluding a container or packaging. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(95), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019; Utah Publication No. 25. “Eating utensils provided by the seller”include plates, knives, forks, spoons, glasses, cups, napkins, or straws. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(95)(a)(iii), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019; Utah Publication No. 25; Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement, as amended through Dec. 14, 2018, Appendix C, Part II. Containers or packaging used to transport food are not considered eating utensils provided by the seller. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(95)(c), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019; Utah Publication No. 25; Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement, as amended through Dec. 14, 2018, Appendix C, Part II. The Utah Tax Commission has stated that a purchase is considered a purchase of prepared food taxable at the full sales tax rate when condiments are made available near vending machines. Utah State Tax Comn., Sales and Use Tax Rate on Grocery Food. Utah does not regard as prepared food any food that the seller only cuts, repackages, or pasteurizes. Raw eggs, raw fish, raw meat, raw poultry or any food containing these raw foods that the FDA recommends should be cooked by the consumer to prevent food borne illness are not considered to be prepared food. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-102(95)(b), as renumbered by 2019 Utah S.B. 168, effective Oct. 1, 2019; Utah Publication No. 25. BNA-SUTN UT 11.2.2.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Definition of 'Restaurant' or 'Eating Establishment'

Defined “Restaurant”is defined as a retail establishment the business of which is the sale of food and beverages for immediate consumption. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-602(5); Utah Publication No. 25. A “restaurant”includes any coffee shop, cafeteria, luncheonette, soda fountain, or fast-food service when food is prepared for immediate consumption. The definition of restaurant does not include theaters, but does include theaters that sell food items. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-602(5); Utah Publication No. 25. BNA-SUTN UT 11.2.3.

Taxable A catering facility is considered a restaurant, and its sales are subject to Utah's sales and use tax, and any county tourism (or restaurant)tax on alcoholic beverages, food and food ingredients and prepared food. Utah Publication No. 55; see Utah Code Ann. §59-12-602(5);Utah Code Ann. §59-12-603(1)(ii). A personal chef is a restaurant subject to the restaurant tax if the personal chef: 1) has a retail establishment; 2) has as its primary business, i.e., 50 percent or more of revenues derive from the sale of food and beverages;and 3) prepares food and beverages for immediate consumption. Utah Private Letter Ruling No. 99-005 (April 26, 1999) (personal chef required to pay restaurant tax on two weeks'worth of prepared meals even though customers do not consume all of the meals at once). BNA-SUTN UT 11.5.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Complimentary Meals

Nontaxable The provider of a complimentary meal is considered the consumer of the items used to prepare the meal. Utah Admin. Code §R865-19S-61(E). The tax commission has decided that when a free meal is provided as part of a frequent buyer program, the provider is not liable for use tax on the ingredients of the meal. Utah Administrative Decision 08-0117 (Jan. 13, 2009) (taxpayer not liable for use tax on ingredients used to prepare free meals redeemed under taxpayer's frequent diner program, even when the redemption is not made in conjunction with the sale of another meal). Restaurants must pay sales or use tax on purchases of ingredients for meals provided free to employees. Utah Publication No. 55. BNA-SUTN UT 11.6.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Mobile Food Vendors

No Guidance Utah does not specifically address the application of sales and use tax to sales by mobile food vendors. Generally, sales of food and food ingredients are subject to a reduced sales tax rate. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(2)(c). Sales of prepared food are taxed at the general rate. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(1)(e). BNA-SUTN UT 11.7.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Restaurant Equipment

Exempt Effective July 1, 2013, sales or leases of tangible personal property between related parties used in the preparation of prepared food are exempt. For the exemption to apply, the seller, purchaser, lessor, or lessee must have paid sales or use tax on the purchase of the tangible personal property prior to making the sale or lease to a related party. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-104(75), as amended by 2013 Utah S.B. 33, effective July 1, 2013. BNA-SUTN UT 11.8.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Vending Machines

Depends Vending machine sales of prepared food, such as sandwiches or pizza, are subject to the general sales tax rate. Sales of grocery food through vending machines, such as packs of gum or bags of pretzels, qualify for the reduced sales tax rate. Utah Publication No. 25; see Utah Admin. Code §R865-19S-74. Sales of food, food ingredients, or prepared food from a vending machine are exempt from sales tax if the proceeds of each sale do not exceed one dollar and the seller or operator of the vending machine reports an amount equal to 150 percent of the cost of food, food ingredients, and prepared foods as goods consumed. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-104(3); Utah Admin. Code §R865-19S-74(B). BNA-SUTN UT 11.9.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Other Food-Related Transactions

Taxable Restaurant Tax In addition to sales and use tax, county governments may impose a tax up to 1 percent on sales of prepared food, food, food ingredients, and alcoholic beverages by restaurants. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-603(1); Utah Publication No. 55. Restaurants and caterers in counties that impose the restaurant tax must charge the additional 1 percent tax on all sales, even if prepared foods are delivered to locations that do not impose the restaurant tax. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-603(1); Utah Publication No. 55. Restaurants and caterers in counties that do not impose the tax do not charge the restaurant tax on any sales. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-603(1); Utah Publication No. 55. The following are subject to both sales tax and restaurant tax:• corkage; • set ups; • required gratuities; • charges for room service meals and beverages. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-603(1); Utah Publication No. 55. The following are not subject to restaurant tax:• restaurant sales of items other than food and beverages, such as souvenir T-shirts; • equipment rental; • use of flowers and decorations; • sales of ice carvings for banquets. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-603(1)(a)(ii); Utah Publication No. 55. A “restaurant” is any retail establishment whose primary business is selling prepared food for immediate consumption. The primary business of an establishment means the business that is the source of more than 50 percent of its revenues. Restaurants subject to this tax include any coffee shop, cafeteria, luncheonette, soda fountain, or fast-food service where food is prepared for immediate consumption. Retail establishments that have a primary business or function of selling fuel or food items for off-premises, but not immediate consumption, and theaters selling food items (excluding dinner theaters)are not considered restaurants for purposes of the tax. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-602(5); Utah Publication No. 55; see also Utah Publication No. 25. A single seller with multiple lines of business at one location is operating multiple retail establishments if the lines of business are not usually regarded as a single retail establishment, or other factors show that the lines of business should be treated separately. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-602(5); Utah Publication No. 55. Local Sales and Use Tax The sale of prepared food is subject to Utah's state sales and use tax. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-103(1)(e). The local sales and use tax applies to the same base as the state sales and use tax. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-204(1); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-204(2)(a)(i); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-204(2)(a)(ii); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-204(6)(a); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-401(1)(a); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-401(3)(a); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-703(1)(a)(i); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-802(1)(a)(i); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-804(1)(a)(i); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-1102(1)(a)(i); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-1302(3)(a); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-1402(1)(a)(i); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-1802(1)(a); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-2103(1)(a)(i); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-2103(1)(a)(ii); Utah Code Ann. §59-12-2213. Therefore, the sale of prepared food is subject to the local sales and use tax. For additional discussion of taxes on meals in Utah, see the Excise Taxes Navigator, at Utah 10.2. Specific Exemptions Airline Food: Sales of food and food ingredients, prepared foods, and alcoholic beverages to a commercial airline carrier for in-flight consumption are exempt from tax. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-104(4). Churches and Charitable Institutions: Sales of food and food ingredients, prepared foods, and alcoholic beverages served by a church or charitable institution and not made available to the general public are exempt from tax. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-104(12)(a)(i), as amended by 2018 Utah S.B. 2001, Second Special Session, effective Jan. 1, 2019; Utah Admin. Code §R865-19S-61. Institutions of Higher Education: Sales of food and food ingredients, prepared foods, and alcoholic beverages served by an institution of higher education are exempt from tax if not made available to the general public or if the item is prepaid as part of a student meal plan offered by the institution. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-104(12)(a)(ii), as amended by 2018 Utah S.B. 2001, Second Special Session, effective Jan. 1, 2019; Utah Admin. Code §R865-19S-61. Medical or Nursing Facilities Sales of food and food ingredients, prepared foods, and alcoholic beverages provided for a patient by a medical or a nursing facility are exempt from sales tax. Utah Code Ann. §59-12-104(12)(b); Utah Admin. Code §R865-19S-61. BNA-SUTN UT 11.10.
