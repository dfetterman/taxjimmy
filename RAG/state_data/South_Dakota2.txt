South Dakota Department of Revenue The South Dakota Department of Revenue administers the sales and use tax in South Dakota. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-46-39; South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018 BNA-SUTN SD 31.1.1.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Agencies Responsible for Administration Local Sales and Use Tax

South Dakota Department of Revenue Municipal Sales and Use Tax South Dakota retailers doing business in a municipality with a municipal sales and use tax must remit the tax to the Department of Revenue, which administers and collects all local sales, use, and gross receipts taxes. Also, all taxable transactions under the municipal sales and use tax must be remitted to the department. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-52-4; S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-52A-3; S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:30; South Dakota Municipal Tax Information Bulletin—January 2019; South Dakota Municipal Tax Information Bulletin—January 2018. Additionally, both state and city taxes should be reported on the same return. S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:29. Municipal sales tax applies when the consumer receives the products or services within a city that imposes a tax. Municipal use tax applies to all products and services when the applicable sales tax is not collected within a city that imposes a tax. South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. The municipal use tax is based on the location where the product or service is used. Additional tax may be due if a product or service is used in a different city that imposes a higher rate of tax than was previously paid. South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. Municipal Gross Receipts Tax The municipal gross receipts tax is to be collected by the seller from the consumer in addition to the municipal sales or use tax. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-52A-1.1; S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-52A-1.2; S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-52A-2. This tax is also administered by the South Dakota Department of Revenue. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-52A-3; South Dakota Municipal Tax Information Bulletin—July 2018; South Dakota Municipal Tax Information Bulletin—January 2018S; South Dakota Municipal Tax Information Bulletin—January 2019. Cities may impose a 1 percent municipal gross receipts tax that is in addition to their sales tax rate on the sale of all or any: alcoholic beverages; eating establishments;lodging accommodations; ticket sales or admissions to places of amusement, athletic, and cultural events. South Dakota Dept. of Rev., Sales and Use Tax Guid —September 2018. Licensed businesses must file on prescribed forms and remit monthly any tax due to the South Dakota Department of Revenue and Regulation on or before the twentieth day of the month following the reporting period. Businesses that file electronically have until the 23rd day following a monthly reporting period to file and until the second to last day of the month following the reporting period to remit the tax. The Secretary of the Department of Revenue may require or allow a taxpayer to file on a non-monthly basis. Furthermore, the Secretary of the Department of Revenue may grant an extension of up to five days to file or remit. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-52A-4.2. Special Jurisdictions Taxes collected in a special jurisdiction must be reported to the state in the same manner as the municipal sales and use tax. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-12A-1; S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-12A-2; S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-12A-4; S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-12A-5; South Dakota Tax Facts—Tribal; South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. Special jurisdictions are assigned a code to be used on tax returns for the remittance of the tax. Once remitted, the tax revenue is distributed between South Dakota and the Tribal governments based on the collection agreement. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-12A-1; S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-12A-2; S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-12A-4; S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-12A-5; South Dakota Municipal Tax Information Bulletin—January 2019; South Dakota Municipal Tax Information Bulletin—July 2018; South Dakota Municipal Tax Information Bulletin - January 2018; South Dakota Tax Facts—Tribal. BNA-SUTN SD 31.1.2.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Vendor/Seller Registration

Required Each retailer or person engaging in a business in the state whose receipts are subject to sales tax must file, with the Department of Revenue, an application for a permit. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-24. Each application must be made on a form prescribed by the Secretary of Revenue and provide the name under which the applicant transacts or intends to transact business, the location of each business, and other information the Secretary of Revenue may require. The applicant must have a permit for each place of business, unless the Secretary of Revenue grants a request for a statewide permit. A statewide permit may be granted if the applicant demonstrates the ability to comply with the filing, auditing, and record-keeping requirements for each location specified in the application. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-24. Promoters Promoters generally provide taxable services and must register for a permit. South Dakota Tax Facts - License Requirements for Sales, Use and Contractors' Excise Tax. Direct Selling Companies Tax is due on sales made by direct selling companies(i.e., multi-level marketing) or their sellers are subject to tax in South Dakota. Generally, the seller must register to collect South Dakota sales tax. However, the Department of Revenue authorizes certain direct selling companies to register to collect and remit tax on behalf of their sellers. Direct sellers making sales in South Dakota should contact the department to determine whether their company has been authorized to collect and remit tax on their sales. If the direct selling company has not been authorized by the department, direct sellers are obligated to register with the department. South Dakota Tax Facts—Direct Selling Companies. Peer-to-Peer Home Renters Persons listing their homes as rental property on websites such as AirBnB, VRBO, HomeAway, FlipKey, and Tripping.com who rent their homes for more than ten days in the calendar year must register with the South Dakota Department of Revenue for a sales tax license. In addition, homeowners may owe use tax on this service fee if the rental company is not licensed to collect South Dakota sales tax. South Dakota Department of Revenue Newsletter - Fall 2016. For more information on the application of sales tax to receipts derived through peer-to-peer home rental websites see the Sales and Use Tax Navigator, beginning at South Dakota 6.11.2. BNA-SUTN SD 31.2.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Responsible Parties Tax Collection and Remittance

Vendor In South Dakota, the retailer is the party responsible for collecting and remitting sales tax. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-2; see also S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-2.7 (noting that a retailer's responsibility to collect sales and use tax is not relieved merely because the taxable sales were processed electronically). Any retailer with a place of business in the state that makes sales or provides services for storage, use, or other consumption in South Dakota must, at the time of making such sale, whether within or without the state, collect the tax from the purchaser and provide a receipt if one is required. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-46-20. Remote Sellers Beginning Nov. 1, 2018, remote sellers who meet certain thresholds must obtain a South Dakota sales tax license and pay applicable sales tax. South Dakota requires any business without a physical presence in South Dakota to obtain a sales tax license and pay applicable sales tax if the business, in the previous or current calendar year, meets one or both of the following:• the business's gross revenue from sales into South Dakota exceeded $100,000; or • the business made sales for delivery into South Dakota in 200 or more separate transactions. South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. South Dakota's Department of Revenue offers a free, online sales tax license application, which can be accessed here: http://sd.gov/taxapp. South Dakota Dept. of Rev., Department of Revenue Offers Additional Guidance on Remote Seller Law (Sept. 18, 2018). Marketplace Providers Beginning March 1, 2019, marketplace providers who meet certain thresholds must obtain a South Dakota sales tax license and pay applicable sales tax on behalf of their marketplace sellers. Marketplace providers are required to remit sales tax on all sales it facilitates into South Dakota if the business, in the previous or current calendar year, meets one or both of the following:• the business's gross revenue from sales into South Dakota exceeded $100,000;or • the business made sales for delivery into South Dakota in 200 or more separate transactions. South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. BNA-SUTN SD 31.3.1.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Responsible Parties Business Owners, Operators

Yes If a taxable entity fails to file required returns or pay the tax due, then South Dakota charges as personally liable the corporate officers, member-managers, managers, or partners who control, supervise, or are charged with filing the returns or remitting tax payments. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-55; South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. The entity's dissolution does not discharge the individual for a prior failure to file or remit. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-55. If the individuals elect not to be personally liable for the failure to file the required returns or to pay the tax due, then the entity must provide the Department of Revenue with a surety bond or certificate of deposit as security for payment of any tax that may become due. The bond or certificate of deposit provided for in this section must be in an amount equal to the estimated annual gross receipts multiplied by the applicable sales or excise tax rate. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-55. BNA-SUTN SD 31.3.3.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Responsible Parties Certified Service Providers

Relieves Liability A seller that contracts with a certified service provider is not liable to the state for sales or use tax due on transactions processed by the certified service provider unless the seller misrepresented the type of items it sells or committed fraud. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45C-7. A certified service provider is the agent of a seller, with whom the certified service provider has contracted, for the collection and remittance of sales and use taxes. As a seller's agent, a certified service provider is liable for sales and use tax due each member state on all sales transactions it processes for the seller except as set out in this section. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45C-7. In the absence of probable cause to believe that the seller has committed fraud or made a material misrepresentation, the seller is not subject to audit on the transactions processed by the certified service provider. A seller is subject to audit for transactions not processed by the certified service provider. The member states acting jointly may perform a system check of the seller and review the seller's procedures to determine if the certified service provider's system is functioning properly and the extent to which the seller's transactions are being processed by the certified service provider. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45C-7. A person that provides a certified automated system is responsible for the proper functioning of that system and is liable to the state for underpayments of tax attributable to errors in the functioning of the certified automated system. A seller that uses a certified automated system remains responsible and is liable to the state for reporting and remitting tax. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45C-7. A seller that has a proprietary system for determining the amount of tax due on transactions and has signed an agreement establishing a performance standard for that system is liable for the failure of the system to meet the performance standard. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45C-7. BNA-SUTN SD 31.3.5.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Due Date Filing

20th of the Month Beginning July 1, 2017, persons that pay electronically must file their returns by the 20th day of the month but have until the 25th day to remit the tax due. The penalty for late filing will be waived until June 30, 2018, however, for those who file by the 23rd day of the month. Also until June 30, 2018, the late payment penalty will be waived for those who pay after the 25th but by the second to last business day of the month. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-27.3, as amended by 2017 S.D. S.B. 36, effective July 1, 2017; South Dakota Department of Revenue Newsletter - Spring 2017. Prior to July 1, 2017, persons subject to sales and use tax must file a return to the South Dakota Department of Revenue on or before the 23rd day of each month. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-27.3. The Secretary may require or allow a person to file a return, and pay any tax due, on a basis other than monthly. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-27.3. Any return, report, or remittance that is required to be filed will be considered timely filed if mailed, postage prepaid, on or before the due date of the reporting period, and is received by the Department. If the due date falls on a Sunday or a legal holiday, then the return is timely filed if mailed, postage prepaid, on the next succeeding day that is not a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday. A U.S. Postal Service postmark is evidence of the date of mailing for the purpose of timely filing of returns, reports, or remittances. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-33. Manufacturing, fabrication, and processing businesses that purchase equipment for business, where the total project's cost exceeds $20,000, may apply for a six month extension on the payment of sales or use tax prior to the start of the project. South Dakota Dept. of Rev., South Dakota's Tax Refund and Tax Delay Payment Programs (July 2014). BNA-SUTN SD 31.5.1.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Exemption Certificates Certificates Required

Yes A seller does not have to collect sales tax if they receive a completed exemption certificate at the time of sale from the purchaser. S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:15; South Dakota Tax Facts—Exemption Certificate; . An exemption certificate is used to:• claim exemption for: ○ products or services intended to be resold;or ○ products or services used for an exempt purpose. • document that the purchaser is an exempt entity. South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. A properly completed exemption certificate has the following information:• the state in which exemption is claimed; • purchaser's name and address; • purchaser's tax identification numbers; • seller's name and address; • purchaser's type of business; • purchaser's reason for exemption; and • purchaser's signature. S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:15; South Dakota Tax Facts—Exemption Certificate. An exemption certificate may be issued for a single purchase or as a blanket certificate. If a purchaser makes recurring purchases (at least one purchase within a period of 12 consecutive months) from the same seller, the purchaser may issue a blanket certificate. The purchaser does not need to provide an exemption certificate for future purchases if it claims an exemption for the same reason. A blanket certificate continues in force until canceled by the purchaser. The purchaser is responsible for assuring that the goods and services purchased exempt are of a type covered by the exemption certificate. S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:15; South Dakota Tax Facts—Exemption Certificate. Penalty Any purchaser that provides an invalid exemption certificate with the intent to evade payment of the tax is guilty of a Class I misdemeanor and may be fined up to 50 percent of the tax in addition to the tax. South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. BNA-SUTN SD 31.8.1.

Until Revoked A blanket certificate is valid until canceled by the purchaser. South Dakota Tax Facts—Exemption Certificate. R. 64:06:01:15; South Dakota Tax Facts—Exemption Certificate. Topic: Administration and Procedure Exemption Certificates Multistate Certificates Multistate Tax Commission (MTC) Certificates

Accepted South Dakota accepts the MTC form. Multistate Tax Commission, Uniform Sales and Use Tax Certificate; see S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:08.01. For the transaction to be a sale for resale, the following conditions must be present: • the service is purchased for or on behalf of a current customer; • the purchaser of the service does not use the service in any manner; and • the service is delivered or resold to the customer without any alteration or change. Multistate Tax Commission, Uniform Sales and Use Tax Certificate; see S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:08.01. BNA-SUTN SD 31.8.3.1.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Exemption Certificates Good Faith Requirement

Yes South Dakota has a good faith requirement. S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:15; South Dakota Tax Facts—Exemption Certificate. Topic: Administration and Procedure Exemption Certificates Direct Pay Permits

Authorized The Secretary may authorize a retailer to use a direct payment permit if the retailer purchases goods or services subject to sales tax. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-46-67. The Department of Revenue indicates that only taxpayers with a sales, use, manufacturer, or wholesaler tax license may qualify for a direct pay permit. South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. A direct payment permit allows the holder to purchase products and services without sales tax. The holder reports and pays the use tax on their purchases directly to the department. Only taxpayers with a sales, use manufacturer, or wholesaler tax license may qualify for a direct payment permit. South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. “Direct payment permit”means a permit issued by the Department that allows a holder of the permit to accrue and pay sales taxes directly to the Department. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-46-67. Applicants for a direct payment permit must apply in writing to the Secretary. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-46-67. The application must meet the following minimum requirements:• demonstrate the applicant's ability to comply with the Department's sales and use tax laws and reporting and payment requirements. The applicant must provide a description of the accounting system used by the applicant and demonstrate that the accounting system reflects the proper amount of tax due; • establish a business purpose for seeking a direct payment permit and must demonstrate that direct payment benefits tax compliance; • the applicant has a volume threshold of annual purchases of tangible personal property and services of at least $3 million, which are subject to sales and use taxes; • the applicant files and pays or agrees to file and pay monthly sales and use tax returns; • the applicant files and pays or agrees to file and pay by electronic means; and • the applicant agrees that its name may be published as a direct payment permit holder on the Department's website and in any other related publication, and may be disclosed as a direct payment permit holder in any other manner for tax administration purposes. S.D. Admin. R. 64:01:01:34(1)-(6). A retailer may appeal the denial of a direct payment permit or contest a revocation of a direct payment permit. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-46-67. Maintain Records A direct payment permit holder must maintain all records necessary for a determination of the correct tax liability. All required records must be made available on request by the Department or its authorized representative. S.D. Admin. R. 64:01:01:35. Payments Each holder of a valid direct payment permit must pay directly to the Department the taxes due for each transaction subject to tax. Taxes for which the direct payment permit is used is considered due and payable on the sales and use tax return next due following the date on which a determination of taxability is made for a given transaction. S.D. Admin. R. 64:01:01:36. Excluded Transactions The holder of a direct pay permit may not use the permit in connection with the following transactions:• purchase of taxable meals or beverages; • purchase of taxable lodging or services related to the lodging; • purchase of admission to places of amusement, entertainment, or athletic events, or the privilege of use of any amusement device; • purchase of any motor vehicle, or other tangible personal property required to be licensed or titled with a taxing authority; • purchase of any of the following enumerated services: telecommunications services, and utilities. Utilities include gas, electricity, and heating fuel;and • any other purchase as may be agreed to in writing between the holder of the direct payment permit and the Department. S.D. Admin. R. 64:01:01:37(1)-(6). Revocation and Transferability A direct payment permit is not transferable, and the use of a direct payment permit may not be assigned to a third party. Direct payment permits may be revoked by the Secretary whenever the Secretary determines that the business holding the permit has not complied with the regulations or that the revocation is in the best interests of the Department. S.D. Admin. R. 64:01:01:41. Seller Liability A retailer that makes a sale to a direct payment permit holder has no liability for sales tax on such sale if the retailer has written evidence of the sale. The written evidence must clearly indicate the name of the buyer, the product or service purchased, and the amount of the purchase. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-46-67; S.D. Admin. R. 64:01:01:38; S.D. Admin. R. 64:01:01:39. BNA-SUTN SD 31.8.10.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Record-Keeping Requirements

3 Years Every person subject to tax must keep for at least three years, records and books of all receipts and sales, together with invoices, bills of lading, copies of bills of sale, and other pertinent papers and documents. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-45. The record-keeping requirement applies to those who pay sales tax or contractor's excise tax, individuals who pay use tax, and any person purchasing products or services for storage, use, or other consumption in South Dakota. South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. Records supporting the following are to be kept and presented upon the department's request:• the amount of gross receipts and sales from all sources, including barter. • the amount of all deductions, exemptions, or credits claimed. South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. Books and records and other papers and documents are, at all times during business hours of the day, subject to inspection in order to determine the amount of tax due. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-45. Books and records must be preserved for the required minimum amount of time unless the Secretary of Revenue, in writing, authorizes their destruction or disposal at an earlier date. However, records pertaining to a depreciating asset must be preserved until the asset is fully depreciated. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-45; S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:35.03. All tax returns, as well as the schedules or working papers issued in connection with the preparation of tax returns, should also be preserved. In addition, records that are generated and maintained in an electronic format are required to be made available to the auditor upon request. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-45; S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:35.03. Anyone who fails to keep the records and books required, or refuses to produce the records to the Department of Revenue for the purpose of examination, could be found guilty of a Class 1 misdemeanor. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-45; S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:35.03. Business records include, but are not limited to, documents pertaining to gross receipts, deductions and use tax as follows. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-45; S.D. Admin. R. 64:06:01:35.03. Gross Receipts Substantiation of gross receipts can be done via the following documents:• sales and billing invoices; • general ledger; • cash register tapes; • bank deposit slips and statements; • sales and/or cash receipts journals; • contracts;or • pricing and portion/service size information menu prices. South Dakota Tax Facts—Audits. Deductions All deductions to gross receipts allowed by law and claimed in filing returns must be supported by the following types of documents:• resale certificate; • proof of exemption (exemption certificate or proof of government funds); • bills of lading or other proof of delivery; • credit memorandums; or • bad debts as claimed on federal income tax returns. South Dakota Tax Facts—Audits. Use Tax Services and use taxable items which are stored, used or otherwise consumed should be identified by:• purchase invoices; • cash disbursement journal or check register; • fixed assets schedule; • inventory withdrawal records; or • depreciation schedules. South Dakota Tax Facts—Audits. BNA-SUTN SD 31.9.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Refunds

Both A taxpayer seeking recovery of an allegedly overpaid tax, penalty, or interest must file a claim for recovery with the Secretary, within three years from the date the tax, penalty, or interest was paid or within three years from the date the return was due, whichever date is earlier. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-19. A claim for recovery not filed within the proper time period is barred. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-19. The Secretary determines the amount of any tax overpayment recoverable by a taxpayer. A taxpayer must provide any information requested or considered necessary by the Secretary to determine the validity of a claim. The Secretary may deny the claim of a taxpayer who fails to provide information requested or considered necessary by the Secretary. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-21. A claim for refund approved by the Secretary will be credited against the future taxes of the taxpayer, except:• a taxpayer having no future tax obligations may receive a refund; or • a taxpayer whose recovery credit is not used within one year may receive a refund equal to the amount of recovery credit unused at the end of the one-year period. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-22. A recovery credit or refund will include interest at the same rate the taxpayer would be charged, except a recovery of an overpayment, resulting from a taxpayer's error, may not include interest. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-24. Refunds for Elderly and Disabled A refund for retail sales and services taxes paid is provided to any person who is a resident of South Dakota for the entire calendar year and who is age 65 or older before Jan. 1 in the year for which a claim for refund is made; or who is disabled. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45A-2. To qualify for the refund, individuals must meet certain household income thresholds. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §§10-45A-5 - 10-45A-6, as amended by 2016 S.D. H.B. 1081, effective Feb. 29, 2016. Claims must be made annually on or before July 1 upon forms prescribed by the Secretary. The deadline may be extended for good cause including sickness, absence, or other disability of the taxpayer. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45A-8. Refunds for Fabricated Tangible Personal Property A contractor or subcontractor may apply for a refund of South Dakota sales and use tax paid by such contractor if:• the sales and use tax subject to the refund request was paid by the contractor or subcontractor requesting the refund for the use of tangible personal property; • the tangible personal property upon which the sales and use tax was paid was purchased by the contractor or subcontractor requesting the refund; • the tangible personal property upon which the sales and use tax was paid was fabricated by the contractor or subcontractor in South Dakota; • the fabricated tangible personal property was used outside of South Dakota by the contractor or subcontractor in the performance of a contract; • the fabricated tangible personal property is not returned to South Dakota;and • the fabricated tangible personal property is exempt from sales or use tax in the state where the contract is performed. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-61-1. Refunds for Small Wind Farms Prime contractors of new and expanded wind generation facilities that produce ten megawatts or less of electricity and cost more than $500,000 are eligible for a refund of 100 percent of contractors'excise tax. The window for the refund is 36 months and there is provision for the refund of sales or use tax. South Dakota Dept. of Rev., South Dakota's Tax Refund and Tax Delay Payment Programs (July 2014). BNA-SUTN SD 31.10.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Penalties Civil Penalties

Yes Any person who is required to file a sales or use tax return or report but fails to file a return or report that includes all taxable transactions within 30 days following the month the return or report is due is subject to an additional amount, assessed as a penalty, equal to 10 percent of the tax or $10, whichever is greater. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-6. Penalties are considered the same as tax for the purposes of collection and enforcement including liens, distress warrants, and criminal violations. Any payment received for taxes, penalty, or interest is applied first to tax, beginning with the oldest delinquency, then to interest and then to penalty. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-6. Penalty for Using Automated Sales Suppression Devices (Phantom-Ware) In South Dakota, a civil penalty of $10,000 applies for each offense where the sales or transaction data is altered because a taxpayer used automated sales suppression or phantom-ware devices. The civil penalty cannot exceed $120,000. Criminal penalties may also apply. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-55, as added by 2016 S.D. H.B. 1051, effective July 1, 2016. BNA-SUTN SD 31.12.1.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Penalties Criminal Penalties

Yes Any person who files a false or fraudulent return while attempting to evade taxes; engages in business after their license is revoked; or engages in business without a license after the being notified by the state that they need a license is guilty of a felony. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-48.1. Any person who fails to remit tax within sixty days of the due date; fails to keep records and books; fails to obtain a license; or violates any rule of the Secretary of Revenue is guilty of a misdemeanor. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-45-48.1. Penalty for Using Automated Sales Suppression Devices (Phantom-Ware) In South Dakota, it is a Class 5 felony to knowingly own, sell, rent, lease, purchase, install, transfer, possess, use, access, design, manufacture or program automated sales suppression devices to alter sales and transaction data. Civil penalties may also apply. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. § 10-59-54, as added by 2016 S.D. H.B. 1051, effective July 1, 2016. BNA-SUTN SD 31.12.2.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Voluntary Disclosure Relief

Yes The South Dakota Department of Revenue and Regulation Business Tax Division administers a Voluntary Disclosure program designed to help businesses or individuals get into compliance with South Dakota's sales and use tax laws. South Dakota Dept. of Rev., Voluntary Disclosure Program. The benefits of the program include possible waiver of penalty and a portion of interest charges, amnesty from criminal prosecution, and 60 days to determine tax liability and prepare returns. South Dakota Dept. of Rev., Voluntary Disclosure Program. To qualify for the program, the taxpayer must not be registered to collect and pay sales or use tax in South Dakota; the taxpayer must not have been contacted by the Department of Revenue and Regulation nor the Multistate Tax Commission to schedule an audit, or to complete a nexus questionnaire; the taxpayer must not be under audit for any South Dakota tax; the taxpayer must not be currently under investigation by any law enforcement agency, and not currently the subject of a civil action or a criminal prosecution involving the sales or use tax covered by the agreement; the taxpayer must agree to register, file returns, and pay the tax due for the look-back period within the time frame specified in the agreement;and the taxpayer must make records available for audit to verify the amount of liability and the accuracy of statements. South Dakota Dept. of Rev., Voluntary Disclosure Program. To apply for the Voluntary Disclosure Program, the taxpayer must submit a written request to the Department. This written request must contain the following information: • a description of activities within the state and when the activities began, including whether the taxpayer owns or leases property in the state, has employees or independent sales representatives soliciting sales in the state, has inventory located in the state, makes deliveries into the state, and engages third parties to install or repair property sold to South Dakota customers; • an estimate of the tax liability for previous tax periods; • a statement of whether South Dakota taxes have been collected; and • a statement that the South Dakota Department of Revenue and Regulation nor the Multistate Tax Commission has contacted the taxpayer. South Dakota Dept. of Rev., Voluntary Disclosure Program. A Voluntary Disclosure Agreement is rendered null and void if it is discovered that there was previous contact with the Department of Revenue and Regulation at any time, misrepresentation of any facts, failure to comply with the terms of the agreement, or if the tax was collected and not remitted. South Dakota Dept. of Rev., Voluntary Disclosure Program. South Dakota provides that taxpayers are not eligible to take advantage of the voluntary disclosure program if:• the taxpayer has had previous contact of any kind with the Department of Revenue; • the taxpayer has misrepresented any facts in the Disclosure Agreement; • the taxpayer fails to comply with the terms of the agreement; • the taxpayer collected and failed to remit the sales and use tax. South Dakota Dept. of Rev., Voluntary Disclosure Program. If the taxpayer is involved in multistate commerce and nexus is a central issue to tax liabilities, the taxpayer has an option to contact the Multistate Tax Commission National Nexus Program. The program's staff works with the taxpayer and the state to resolve tax disputes at no cost to the taxpayer. Multistate Tax Commission, Multistate Voluntary Disclosure Program . BNA-SUTN SD 31.15.1.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Voluntary Disclosure Multistate Tax Commission (MTC) Program

Participates South Dakota participates in the Multistate Voluntary Disclosure Program(MVDP). Multistate Tax Commission, Multistate Voluntary Disclosure Program. The MVDP allows taxpayers with outstanding tax liabilities in multiple states to negotiate settlements via a process coordinated by the National Nexus Program (NNP) staff of the MTC. Multistate Tax Commission, Multistate Voluntary Disclosure Program. To participate in the MVDP, the taxpayer must:• have a minimum good-faith tax liability estimate of $500 or more; • not have been contacted by a state or commission regarding taxes due;and • submit a completed Voluntary Disclosure Agreement (VDA) including taxpayer's contact information, tax year, list of states to receive the proposed VDA, description of taxpayer's activities in each state, and estimate of taxes due. Multistate Tax Commission, Multistate Voluntary Disclosure Program. South Dakota may have additional requirements for participation in the MVDP. The program is intended to incentivize taxpayers to begin filing and paying taxes in all of the states in which they have substantial nexus. Upon entering into a voluntary disclosure agreement with a state, taxpayers will be required to file returns, pay the tax due and accrued interest on the returns, and register with the state in exchange for waiver of penalties for the entire look-back period. Taxpayers are not charged to participate in the MVDP. Online Marketplace Seller Voluntary Disclosure Initiative - Special MTC Program From Aug. 17, 2017, through Nov. 1, 2017, South Dakota is participating in the MTC's special limited-time voluntary disclosure initiative in which back sales and use tax liability may be waived for eligible online marketplace sellers that timely register and begin to collect the state's sales and use tax. For more information about this initiative, see Portfolio 1300-2nd: Sales and Use Taxes: General Principles. BNA-SUTN SD 31.15.3.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Audits

Authorized The Secretary may investigate any taxpayer or business (licensed or unlicensed)and examine relevant records, including applicable licensing, the filing and correctness of returns, and the payment of taxes, interest, and penalties. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-5; South Dakota Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. Unless the Secretary determines that delay may jeopardize the collection of a tax, the Secretary must mail a notice of intent to audit at least 30 days before commencement of the audit to the person to be audited. The 30-day period may be waived by mutual consent of both parties. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-7. A “notice of intent to audit” is any notice mailed to any taxpayer indicating that an auditor intends to perform an audit of the books and records of or concerning a taxpayer to determine whether the proper amount of tax, penalty, or interest has been paid. The notice indicates the type of tax that is the subject of the audit and the date of commencement of the audit. The notice also contains the statement “All records, books, and documents must be prepared for presentation to the auditor on the date of commencement of the audit. All documents evidencing reduction, deduction, or exemption of tax not prepared for presentation within 60 days of the date of commencement of the audit need not be considered by the auditor.” S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-3. Any documents or records required to be kept by law to evidence reduction, deduction, or exemption from tax not prepared for presentation to the auditor within 60 days from the commencement date of the audit do not have to be considered by the auditor or the Secretary. However, additional pertinent papers or documents must be considered if all the following apply: S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-7. • the additional pertinent papers or documents are material; • there were good reasons for failure to present other pertinent papers or documents within the prescribed time period; and • the additional pertinent papers or documents are submitted within a reasonable time period prior to any hearing with the Secretary scheduled pursuant to S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-9. BNA-SUTN SD 31.16.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Appeals

Permitted Any person aggrieved or interested may appeal to the circuit court from any decision of the Secretary of Revenue, or of the director of any division of the Department of Revenue, or either or any or all of them, relating to licensing, revocation of license, compromise, or adjustment of taxes, or refund of taxes. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-1-41. Eligible parties include any officer, department, board, commission, agency, institution, or subdivision of the state. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-1-41. BNA-SUTN SD 31.17.

Topic: Administration and Procedure Interest Underpayment Rates 2019

1Q:1% monthly 2Q:1% monthly 3Q:1% monthly 4Q:1% monthly In 2019, the South Dakota interest rate on underpayment of tax is the greater of 1 percent or $5 for the first month, and 1 percent per month thereafter. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-6. For failure to pay tax with the intent to intentionally avoid or delay the payment, the underpayment interest rate is the greater of 1.5 percent or $5 per month. S.D. Codified Laws Ann. §10-59-6. BNA-SUTN SD 31.13.2.12.
