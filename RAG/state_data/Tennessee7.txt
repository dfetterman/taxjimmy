Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Definition of 'Food and Food Ingredients'

Defined “Food and food ingredients” means any substance, whether liquid, concentrated, solid, frozen, dried, or dehydrated form, sold for human ingestion or chewing. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(41) (effective July 1, 2021, the definition for “food and food ingredients”is located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(43)); 2019 Tenn. H.B. 931, effective April 12, 2019 (extending Tennessee's compliance with SSUTA until July 1, 2021). Food is consumed for its taste or nutritional value, and does not include alcoholic beverages, prepared food, or tobacco. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(41) (effective July 1, 2021, the definition for “food and food ingredients”is located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(43)); 2019 Tenn. H.B. 931, effective April 12, 2019 (extending Tennessee's compliance with SSUTA until July 1, 2021). Tennessee incorporates the definition of food and food ingredients as found in the Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-228; Tennessee Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. Examples of food and food ingredients are baby food, bottled water, bread, canned foods, cereal, chips, dips, coffee, condiments, eggs, raw fish, meat, or poultry, fruit, herbs and spices, ice cream, lunch meat, uncooked pasta, salad dressings, soft drinks, trail mix, vegetables, and yogurt. Tennessee Dept. of Rev., State Sales Tax Rate on Food Now 4%. BNA-SUTN TN 11.1.2.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Candy

Taxable Tennessee imposes the sales tax on candy at the full rate. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-228(b); Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-202(a) as amended by 2019 Tenn. H.B. 931, effective April 12, 2019 (extending Tennessee's compliance with SSUTA until July 1, 2021); Tennessee Notice No. 17-22 (April 2017); Streamlined Sales Tax Governing Board, Taxability Matrix, Tennessee (version 2018.0); Tennessee Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. Candy is a preparation of sugar, honey, or other natural or artificial sweetener combined with chocolate, fruit, nuts, or other ingredients or flavorings in the form of bars, drops, or pieces. However, candy does not include a preparation that contains flour or that requires refrigeration. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(9) (effective July 1, 2021, the definition for “candy” is located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(10)). See Tennessee Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. A candy preparation is made by means of heating, coloring, molding or otherwise processing any of the above ingredients. Tennessee Notice No. 17-22 (April 2017). Candy must be in the form of bars, drops, or pieces. A bar is a product sold in a square, oblong, or similar shape, and a drop is a product that is sold in a round, oval, pear, or similar shape. A piece is a smaller portion of a product that has the same make-up as the product as a whole. Tennessee Notice No. 17-22 (April 2017). A natural or artificial sweetener is an ingredient that adds a sugary sweetness to the taste of the food product. Examples of this include corn syrup, dextrose, invert sugar, sucrose, fructose, sucralose, saccharin, aspartame, stevia, fruit juice concentrates, molasses, evaporated cane juice, rice syrup, barley malt honey, maltitol, and agave. Tennessee Notice No. 17-22 (April 2017). Sweet and semi-sweet baking chips are taxable as candy. Tennessee Dept. of Rev., State Sales Tax Rate on Food Now 4%. BNA-SUTN TN 11.1.3.2.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Food and Food Ingredients Specific Products Dietary Supplements and Vitamins

Code Ann. §67-6-102(28)(A)); Tennessee Notice No. 17-23 (October 2017). Additionally, to qualify as a dietary supplement, the product must also be intended for ingestion in tablet, capsule, powder, soft-gel, gel-cap, or liquid form, or, if not intended for ingestion in such forms, is not represented as conventional food and is not represented for use as the sole item of a meal or diet; furthermore, the product is required to be labeled as a dietary supplement as required under 21 C.F.R. 101.36. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(26)(B) (effective July 1, 2021, the definition for “dietary supplement”is located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(28)(B)); Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(26)(C) (effective July 1, 2021, the definition for “dietary supplement”is located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(28)(C)). BNA-SUTN TN 11.1.3.3.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Prepared Food Definition of 'Prepared Food'

Defined “Prepared food” is food and food ingredients in a heated state, or heated by the retailer; two or more food ingredients mixed or combined by the seller for sale as a single item; or food sold with eating utensils provided by the retailer, including plates, knives, forks, spoons, glasses, cups, napkins, or straws. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(66)(A)(i) through Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(66)(A)(iii); Tennessee Notice No. 17-21 (October 2017). Effective July 1, 2021, the definition for “prepared food” will be located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(68)(A)(iii). See 2019 Tenn. H.B. 931, effective April 12, 2019 (extending Tennessee's compliance with SSUTA until July 1, 2021). Prepared food does not include:• food and food ingredients that are only sliced, repackaged, or pasteurized by a retailer; or • fish, meat, and poultry, and foods containing any fish, meats, or poultry in raw form that require cooking by the consumer, as recommended by the Food and Drug Administration to prevent food bourne illnesses. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(66)(B) (effective July 1, 2021, explanation items not considered “prepared food”will be located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(68)(B)); Tennessee Notice No. 17-21 (October 2017). Food is sold in a heated state if it is sold at a higher temperature than the air temperature of the place where it is sold. Food is considered heated by the seller if the seller previously heated the item, even if it is not heated at the time of sale. Tennessee Notice No. 17-21 (October 2017). Food is considered to contain two or more ingredients mixed together by the seller if the seller mixes ingredients together to create a new food item to sell. The requirement is also met if the seller combines multiple items and sells them as one item, even if a new distinct food item is not created. Tennessee Notice No. 17-21 (October 2017). Eating utensils include, but are not limited to, plates, knives, forks, spoons, glasses, napkins, cups, and straws. If the manufacturer, rather than the seller, provides an eating utensil with the food, it is defined as food or food ingredients and it is taxable at the lower rate. Tennessee Notice No. 17-21 (October 2017). Eating utensils are provided by the seller if the seller's practice is to:• physically give or hand the utensils to the purchaser, or • make the utensils available on the premises, but only if the seller's percentage of food that is otherwise considered prepared food is more than 75 percent of all food sales. Tennessee Notice No. 17-21 (October 2017). Prepared food does not include food that the seller only cuts, repackages, or pasteurizes. For example, deli meats or cheeses that the seller slices and repackages for sale to the customer or potato salad that the seller does not make and only repackages into smaller containers and sells in the deli are not prepared food. Tennessee Notice No. 17-21 (October 2017). Prepared food includes bakery goods, fruit trays, coffee or tea, ice cream served in cones, party trays, ready to eat meats, salad greens mixed by the seller, sandwiches, soups or meals sold warm and ready to eat, and warmed nuts. Tennessee Notice No. 17-21 (October 2017). BNA-SUTN TN 11.2.2.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Catering and Personal Chefs

Taxable If a caterer or personal chef serves food and food ingredients or prepared food, then said items are taxable, either at the lower rate of 5 percent or the regular rate at 7 percent, plus local tax. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-228; Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-202(a); Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(66)(A); Tennessee Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. Effective July 1, 2021, the definition for “prepared food” will be located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(68)(A). See 2019 Tenn. H.B. 931, effective April 12, 2019 (extending Tennessee's compliance with SSUTA until July 1, 2021). Optional services provided, such as service by waiters, bartenders, bar backs, show chefs, valets, etc., are severable from catering contracts. Tomkats Catering, Inc. v. Johnson (Tenn. Ct. App. 2001). BNA-SUTN TN 11.5.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Complimentary Meals

Taxable When a hotel provides complimentary food or beverages to individuals who are not registered guests, the hotel is considered the user and consumer of the food and beverages purchased. It is using these items performing its room rental service and is; therefore, liable for sales and use tax when purchasing the food and beverages for the complimentary meals. Tennessee Notice No. 07-10 (Nov. 2007). BNA-SUTN TN 11.6.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Mobile Food Vendors

Taxable Tennessee's sales and use tax laws do not specifically address mobile food vendors;however, if the mobile food vendor serves food and food ingredients or prepared food, then the items are taxable, either at the lower rate of 5 percent, or the regular rate at 7 percent, plus local tax. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-228; Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-202(a); Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(66)(A). Effective July 1, 2021, the definition for “prepared food” will be located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(68)(A). See 2019 Tenn. H.B. 931, effective April 12, 2019 (extending Tennessee's compliance with SSUTA until July 1, 2021). BNA-SUTN TN 11.7.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Restaurant Equipment

Depends Generally, Tennessee imposes the sales and use tax on the retail sale, use, or consumption of tangible personal property; thus, it follows that any tangible personal property used or consumed in the preparation of food, meals, or beverages will be subject to sales and use tax. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-202; Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-203 as amended by 2019 Tenn. H.B. 931, effective April 12, 2019 (extending Tennessee's compliance with SSUTA until July 1, 2021); Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(89)(A). Effective July 1, 2021, the definition for “tangible personal property” is located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(91)(A). See 2019 Tenn. H.B. 931, effective April 12, 2019 (extending Tennessee's compliance with SSUTA until July 1, 2021). Machinery used in preparing food for immediate retail sale is not “industrial machinery.” Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(44) (effective July 1, 2021, the definition for “prepared food” is located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-102(46)). BNA-SUTN TN 11.8.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Vending Machines

Taxable Generally, vending machine sales are subject to Tennessee's sales tax. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-202(c) as amended by 2019 Tenn. H.B. 931, effective April 12, 2019 (extending Tennessee's compliance with SSUTA until July 1, 2021); Tennessee Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018. Moreover, these sales are subject to the state general tax rate or reduced food tax rate plus a standard rate of 2.25 percent local tax. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-702(h) (Effective beginning July 1, 2021, this provision is located in Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-702(d); Tennessee Sales and Use Tax Guide—September 2018; Tennessee Notice 17-17 (Aug. 2017). However, if a vending machine is operated for the benefit of a charitable nonprofit organization, dispenses merchandise with a market value of 25 cents or less, and is built so that only a fixed, predetermined price can be paid for an item, then the vending machine operator may choose to pay a 1.5 percent gross receipts tax on the sales, rather than paying the sales tax. Tennessee Notice 17-17 (Aug. 2017). BNA-SUTN TN 11.9.

Topic: Food and Food Products, Eating Establishments Other Food-Related Transactions

Depends Tennessee's sales and use tax laws vary in their treatment of the provision of food or meals by boardinghouses, transportation facilities, schools, and businesses to their employees. Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.34; Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.36; Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.35; Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.18. Meals Served to Boarders When meals are provided by a boardinghouse, but only to boarders and not the general public, the operator of the boardinghouse is considered the end consumer of food purchased and is responsible for paying sales tax on the food. Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.34(2); Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.36. Meals Served by Railroads, Airplanes, or Other Transportation Facilities Sales of prepared food by railroads, on airplanes, or in other transportation facilities are subject to sales tax at the regular rate of 7 percent. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-228(b); Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.35(1). However, when transportation facilities provide food to customers as part of the ticket price, the transportation provider is considered the end user of the food purchased. If the food is purchased in Tennessee, sales tax is due on the purchase regardless of where the food is actually served. Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.35(2). School Lunches The sale of food to schools or school organizations for the purpose of preparing school lunches for students is exempt from sales tax. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-229; Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.36. “School lunches” are defined as lunches served to students in public or private school, so long as the student is in kindergarten through 12th grade. Tenn. Code Ann. §67-6-229; Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.36. Sales of Meals to Employees An employer who sells meals to employees must charge sales tax on the transaction. Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.18(1). Meals sold to employees are taxed based on the charge made to the employee by the employer, or the cost of the ingredients in the meal, whichever is greater. Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. §1320-5-1-.18(2). BNA-SUTN TN 11.10.
