State: Maine



Topic: Imposition of Tax General Rule - Imposition of Tax Sales Tax

Yes Maine imposes a sales tax on the value of all tangible personal property and taxable services sold at retail. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1811. The sales tax is imposed at a special rate on the value of liquor that is sold in licensed establishments, the value of the rental of living quarters in any hotel, rooming house, or tourist or trailer camp, the value of an automobile rented for less than one year, and the value of prepared foods. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1811. Under Maine law, a service provider tax is imposed on the value of specific services sold in the state of Maine. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §2552(1). This tax is in lieu of the sales and use tax with regard to certain enumerated services. The value of the services is measured by the sales price of the service. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §2552(2). This tax is imposed upon, and is a levy on the seller, not on the purchaser. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §2552(2). A seller is permitted to pass this tax on to a customer, provided that on the customer's bill the tax is shown as a separate line item and identified as the service provider tax. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §2552(2); Maine Instructional Bulletin No. 55. BNA-SUTN ME 1.1.1.

Topic: Imposition of Tax General Rule - Imposition of Tax Use Tax

Yes Maine imposes a use tax on the value of tangible personal property and taxable services that are the object of storage, use, or other consumption in Maine. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1861. BNA-SUTN ME 1.1.2.

Topic: Imposition of Tax General Rule - Imposition of Tax Local Option Sales and Use Tax

No Local Tax The Maine statutes relating to taxation and the Maine Constitution currently do not provide for the imposition of any sales taxes by local jurisdictions. See Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1811. Maine's tax laws also do not provide for the imposition of use taxes by local jurisdictions. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1861. BNA-SUTN ME 1.1.3.

Topic: Imposition of Tax General Rule - Imposition of Tax Excise and Replacement Taxes

Yes Excise Taxes Maine's tax law provides for a number of “special taxes” that are to be applied to various specified sales of tangible personal property and taxable services or the use, storage, or consumption of tangible personal property or taxable services in Maine. See, e.g., Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §4303. These special taxes include, but are not limited to, Maine taxes that are codified and known as the “blueberry tax,” “cigarette tax,” and “mahogany quahog tax.” Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §4303; Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §4365; Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §4712. For more information about excise taxes in Maine, see the Excise Tax Navigator, beginning at Maine 1.1. Replacement Taxes The Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement prohibits member states from imposing a “prohibited replacement tax” which is a tax outside of the sales and use tax that is imposed on products defined in the Agreement. Generally, the Streamlined Sales Tax Governing Board will make a finding regarding whether or not a member state has imposed a replacement tax. Maine is not a member of the Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement. Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Governing Board, State Info; Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement as amended through December 16, 2016. BNA-SUTN ME 1.1.4.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Historical Information

Est. 1951 Maine's sales and use tax was first imposed on July 1, 1951 at the rate of 2 percent. Maine Reference Guide to the Sales and Use Tax Law. It has since varied in its general rate from 3 percent to 6 percent. Maine Reference Guide to the Sales and Use Tax Law. The service provider tax was first imposed in 2004. Maine Reference Guide to the Sales and Use Tax Law. In June 2009, Maine enacted legislation (the Maine Tax Reform Law) that would have broadened the sales tax base to include additional services such as amusement, entertainment, and recreation services. However, the Maine Tax Reform law enacted in 2009 was repealed by a majority of voters at the June 8, 2010 referendum. As a result, changes to the sales tax laws scheduled to go into effect in 2010, 2011, and 2012 did not go into effect. Maine General Information Bulletin No. 99; Maine General Information Bulletin No. 100. BNA-SUTN ME 1.2.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Pending and Trending Developments

Exemptions Effective October 1, 2016, Maine enacted new and expanded sales tax exemptions for prosthetic and orthotic devices, as well as an expanded exemption for all fuel used in commercial farming, commercial fishing, commercial aquaculture, and commercial wood harvesting, effective January 1, 2017. Maine General Instructional Bulletin No. 106. Whether Maine will expand its sales tax exemptions to include durable medical equipment or mobility enhancing devices remains an open question. Maine General Instructional Bulletin No. 106. BNA-SUTN ME 1.3.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Key Definitions 'Sale' and 'Retail Sale'

Defined The term “sale” means any transfer, exchange, or barter, in any manner or by any means whatsoever, for consideration, and includes leases and contracts payable by rental or license fees for the right of possession and use, but only when the State Tax Assessor deems leases and contracts to be in lieu of purchase. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(13). Maine also defines the term “retail sale.” In Maine, a “retail sale”is any sale of tangible personal property or a taxable service in the ordinary course of business. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(11). A “retail sale” includes:• conditional sales, installment lease sales, and any other transfer of tangible personal property when the title is retained as security for the payment of the purchase price and is intended to be transferred later; • sales of products for internal human consumption to a person for resale through vending machines when sold to a person more than 50 percent of whose gross receipts from the retail sale of tangible personal property are derived from sales through vending machines. The tax must be paid by the retailer to the state; • a sale in the ordinary course of business by a retailer to a purchaser who is not engaged in selling that kind of tangible personal property or taxable service in the ordinary course of repeated and successive transactions of like character; and • the sale or liquidation of a business or the sale of substantially all of the assets of a business, to the extent that the seller purchased the assets of the business for resale, lease or rental in the ordinary course of business, except when the sale is to: ○ an affiliated entity and the transferee, or ultimate transferee in a series of transactions among affiliated entities that purchases the assets for resale, lease or rental in the ordinary course of business; or ○ a person that purchases the assets for resale, lease or rental in the ordinary course of business or that purchases the assets for transfer to an affiliate, directly or through a series of transactions among affiliated entities, for resale, lease or rental by the affiliate in the ordinary course of business. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(11)(A). For purposes of this subparagraph, “affiliate”or “affiliated” includes both direct and indirect affiliates. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(11)(A). A “retail sale” does not include:• any casual sale; • any sale by a personal representative in the settlement of an estate, unless the sale is made through a retailer or made in the continuation or operation of a business; • the sale, to a person engaged in the business of renting automobiles, of automobiles, integral parts of automobiles, or accessories to automobiles, for rental or for use in an automobile rented for a period of less than one year. For purposes of this subparagraph, “automobile” includes a pickup truck or van with a gross vehicle weight of less than 26,000 pounds; • the sale, to a person engaged in the business of renting video media and video equipment, of video media or video equipment for rental; • the sale, to a person engaged in the business of renting or leasing automobiles, of automobiles for rental or lease for one year or more; • the sale, to a person engaged in the business of providing cable or satellite television services or satellite radio services, of associated equipment for rental or lease to subscribers in conjunction with a sale of cable or satellite television services or satellite radio services; Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(11)(B)(1) through Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(11)(B)(6), as amended by 2016 Me. S.P. 607, §5, effective March 10, 2016. • the sale, to a person engaged in the business of renting furniture, audio media, or audio equipment, of furniture, audio media, or audio equipment for rental pursuant to a rental-purchase agreement; • the sale of loaner vehicles to a new vehicle dealer licensed as such; • the sale of automobile repair parts used in the performance of repair services on an automobile pursuant to an extended service contract sold on or after Sept. 20, 2007, that entitles the purchaser to specific benefits in the service of the automobile for a specific duration; • the sale, to a retailer that has been issued a resale certificate of tangible personal property for resale in the form of tangible personal property, except resale as a casual sale; • the sale, to a retailer that has been issued a resale certificate of a taxable service for resale, except resale as a casual sale; • the sale, to a retailer that is not required to register, of tangible personal property for resale outside the state in the form of tangible personal property, except resale as a casual sale; • the sale, to a retailer that is not required to register, of a taxable service for resale outside the state, except resale as a casual sale; • the sale of repair parts used in the performance of repair services on telecommunications equipment pursuant to an extended service contract that entitles the purchaser to specific benefits in the service of the telecommunications equipment for a specific duration; • the sale of positive airway pressure equipment and supplies for rental for personal use to a person engaged in the business of renting positive airway pressure equipment; • the sale, to a person engaged in the business of renting or leasing motor homes or camper trailers, of motor homes or camper trailers for rental;or • the sale of truck repair parts used in the performance of repair services on a truck pursuant to an extended service contract that entitles the purchaser to specific benefits in the service of the truck for a specific duration. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(11)(B)(7) through Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(11)(B)(17). BNA-SUTN ME 1.4.1.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Key Definitions 'Lease'

Not Defined Maine does not define “lease.” See Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752. However, the state specifies certain circumstances that create a lease “in lieu of purchase.” Maine Instructional Bulletin No. 20. A lease will be considered a lease “in lieu of purchase” under the following circumstances:• the terms of the lease create a security interest, contains an option to purchase the leased property for $1.00 or other nominal consideration; • the lessee assumes responsibility for the disposition of the property at the end of the lease term; or • the lease is a so-called T.R.A.C. (Terminal Rental Adjustment Clause)lease. See Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752; Maine Instructional Bulletin No. 20. A lease determined to be a lease “in lieu of purchase” is treated as a sale for tax purposes. The sale occurs at the commencement of the lease. Lessors are encouraged to contact Maine Revenue Services when uncertain whether a particular lease constitutes a lease in lieu of purchase. Maine Instructional Bulletin No. 20. BNA-SUTN ME 1.4.2.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Key Definitions 'Use' or 'Storage, Use, or Consumption'

Defined The term “use” includes the exercise within Maine of any right or power over tangible personal property incident to its ownership, including storage of the property and the derivation of income from the rental of the property, whether received in money or in the form of other benefits. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(21), as amended by 2019 Me. H.P. 1294, effective Sept. 19, 2019. “Use” includes a retailer's removal from inventory of tangible personal property for which sales tax was not imposed at the time of purchase. Maine Business Guide to Sales, Use and Service Provider Tax. “Use” does not include keeping, retaining or exercising power over tangible personal property brought into the State for the purpose of subsequently transporting it outside the State for use by the purchaser thereafter solely outside the State or for the purpose of being processed, fabricated, manufactured or incorporated into or attached to other tangible personal property to be transported outside the State and thereafter used by the purchaser solely outside the State. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(21), as amended by 2019 Me. H.P. 1294, effective Sept. 19, 2019. BNA-SUTN ME 1.4.3.

Topic: Imposition of Tax Key Definitions 'Tangible Personal Property'

Defined In Maine, “tangible personal property” means personal property that can be seen, weighed, measured, felt, touched, or in any other manner perceived by the senses. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(17), as added by 2014 Me. S.P. 673, effective Aug. 1, 2014. The term includes electricity, non-custom computer software, and products transferred electronically. However, it does not include rights and credits, insurance policies, bills of exchange, stocks and bonds, or similar evidences of indebtedness or ownership. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann. tit. 36, §1752(17), as added by 2014 Me. S.P. 673, effective Aug. 1, 2014 (adding “products transferred electronically”). BNA-SUTN ME 1.4.4.
