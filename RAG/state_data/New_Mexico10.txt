State: New Mexico



Topic: Jurisdiction and Sourcing Rules Sourcing Rules Tangible Personal Property

Delivery Location Effective July 1, 2021, sales and leases of tangible personal property are sourced to the location of delivery of that tangible personal property to the customer, provided that the reporting location for receipts from leasing a vehicle is the location where the customer first makes use of the vehicle. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14(A), as amended by 2019 N.M. H.B. 6, §11, effective July 1, 2021; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(7); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-200; New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. Prior to July 1, 2021, generally, New Mexico imposes gross receipts tax upon a person doing business within the state at a combined state and local rate. The business location of that person determines the local rate of tax to be imposed upon the transaction at issue. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(7); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-200; New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. Sales at an out-of-state location where the customer picks up the property at that out-of-state location are not taxable in New Mexico. New Mexico Public Decision 17-37 (Sept. 13, 2017) (a customer having a billing address in New Mexico is not sufficient to establish that delivery or sale took place in New Mexico). Multiple Business Locations If a person has more than one place of business in New Mexico, the Department will accept, on audit, the person's method of crediting sales to each place of business, provided the method of crediting is in accordance with the person's regular accounting practice and contains no obvious distortion. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-200; New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. Itinerant Peddlers An itinerant peddler is required to report taxable gross receipts by the municipality or the area of a county outside any municipality where the peddler maintains a place of business. If the itinerant peddler sells from only one location, that location will be considered the place of business for sourcing purposes. If an individual peddler has no set sales location, the place of business will be the peddler's temporary or permanent residence within New Mexico. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-200. An itinerant peddler is a person, with no employees that does no advertising or soliciting in the state that sells property from a nonreserved location chosen for temporary periods on a first-come, first-served basis. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-200. BNA-SUTN NM 4.1.1.

Topic: Jurisdiction and Sourcing Rules Sourcing Rules Services

Business Location New Mexico imposes gross receipts tax upon a person doing business within the state at a combined state and local rate. Effective July 1, 2021, services other than professional services, construction services, and selling real estate are sourced to the location where the service is performed. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14(A)(2), as amended by 2019 N.M. H.B. 6, §11, effective July 1, 2021. For persons engaged in the construction business, the place where the construction project is performed is a “place of business,” and all receipts from that project are to be reported from that place of business. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14(A)(2), as amended by 2019 N.M. H.B. 6, §11, effective July 1, 2021. Effective July 1, 2021, for a person engaged in the business of providing services with respect to the selling of real estate, the location of the real property is the “place of business,” and all gross receipts and deductions from that sale are to be reported from that place of business. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14(A)(2), as amended by 2019 N.M. H.B. 6, §11, effective July 1, 2021. Effective July 1, 2021, gross receipts and deductions from the sale, lease or granting of a license to use real property must be reported to the location of the real property. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14(A)(2), as amended by 2019 N.M. H.B. 6, §11, effective July 1, 2021. Effective July 1, 2021, the reporting location for gross receipts and deductions from a customer for services provided by a transportation network company is the location where the customer enters the vehicle offered for a prearranged ride. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14(A)(2), as amended by 2019 N.M. H.B. 6, §11, effective July 1, 2021. Prior to July 1, 2021, the business location of that person determines the local rate of tax to be imposed upon the transaction at issue. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(G); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-200; New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. For sellers of taxable services, business location may also be considered the space provided to the seller by the purchaser of the services if all of the following conditions are present: • the space is occupied by the provider of the service for a period of six consecutive months or longer; • critical elements of the service are performed at, managed or coordinated from the purchaser's location; and • the provider or employees of the provider of the service are expected, by the purchaser of the services or representatives of the purchaser, to be available at that location during established times. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(G). If it is unclear whether the seller of services meets the three criteria above, the following criteria will be considered to determine the business location of a seller of services: • whether the provider of the service has assigned employees to the client's location as a condition of employment; • whether a telephone is assigned for the exclusive use by the service provider; • whether the space has been designated for the use of the service provider; • whether the space contains office furniture or equipment furnished by either the client or the service provider for the sole use of the service provider; • whether the service provider is identified by business name on a sign located in or adjacent to the provided space; • whether clients or other persons can expect to communicate, either in person or by telephone, with the service provider or employees or representatives of the service provider at the space provided by the client; and • whether the contract between the client and the service provider requires the client to provide space to the service provider. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(G)(2). Gross receipts from services performed out-of-state, other than research and development services, are exempt from New Mexico's gross receipts tax. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-9-13.1(A). Example: Barry Black is routinely hired by the Drug Enforcement Agency to handle hazardous chemicals and materials associated with clandestine labs. Barry identifies, packages, labels, and removes the items from the labs, sending them to hazardous material disposal facilities in other states. Result: Barry is performing a taxable hazardous waste removal service in New Mexico. Although part of the service is completed out-of-state the primary objective of the taxpayer is the waste removal service, which occurs within New Mexico. New Mexico Public Decision No. 18-42 (Dec. 3, 2018). BNA-SUTN NM 4.1.2.

Topic: Jurisdiction and Sourcing Rules Sourcing Rules Leases and Rentals

Delivery Location Effective July 1, 2021, leases of tangible personal property, are sourced to the location of delivery of that tangible personal property to the customer, provided that the reporting location for receipts from leasing a vehicle is the location where the customer first makes use of the vehicle. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14(A), as amended by 2019 N.M. H.B. 6, §11, effective July 1, 2021; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(7); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-200; New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. Prior to July 1, 2021, persons engaged in the business of leasing tangible personal property in New Mexico are required to source their sales to their business location regardless of where the property is used. New Mexico Bulletin FYI-200. An in-state person engaged in the business of leasing with more than one place of business is required to report gross receipts for each municipality or area within a county outside of any municipality in which the person maintains a place of business. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(C). A person from out-of-state who is engaged in the business of leasing as defined in N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-9-3(E) and who has no place of business or resident sales personnel in New Mexico is required to indicate “out-of-state” on the CRS-1 report form and to calculate gross receipts tax due using the tax rate for the state. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(C); New Mexico Public Decision 17-37 (Sept. 13, 2017). Additionally, an out-of-state person engaged in the business of leasing who has a place of business or resident sales personnel in New Mexico is required to report gross receipts for each municipality or area within a county outside of any municipalities in which the person maintains a place of business or resident sales personnel. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(C). BNA-SUTN NM 4.1.3.

Topic: Jurisdiction and Sourcing Rules Sourcing Rules Local Sourcing

Business Location Generally, county and local sourcing in New Mexico depends on the business location from which the goods are sold or the services are provided. New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. For tax reporting purposes, New Mexico has provided the following guidelines for determining the applicable local tax rate:• If the business is located in a municipality within a county; the municipal rate applies. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. • If the business is located outside any incorporated municipality, the applicable county rate applies. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. • If the business has multiple stores within one municipality or county, there is only one business location for reporting purposes. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. • If the business has multiple business locations under one identification number, report receipts for each location separately. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. • If the business has a resident salesperson, the business location is the location of the salesperson. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. • If the business is a construction business: the tax rate is determined by the location of each construction project. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. • If the business sells real estate, the business location is the location of each property sold. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. • If the business is a utility, the tax rate is determined by the location of the meter used to record the amount of service consumed by the customer or the location of the telephone set. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13(A)(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. • If the business provides cellular service, it is the location of the customer's place of primary use. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13.A(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. • If the business is a craft person who rents a fixed booth at a crafts fair, the booth is a business location and the tax rate is based on the location of the craft fair. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-1-14; N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §1.4.13.A(6); New Mexico Bulletin FYI-105. BNA-SUTN NM 4.1.4.

Topic: Jurisdiction and Sourcing Rules Credit for Tax Paid to Another Jurisdiction

Yes New Mexico allows a reciprocal use tax credit up to the amount of any sales or use tax paid to another state. N.M. Stat. Ann. § 7-9-79(A); N.M. Stat. Ann. § 7-5-1, Art. V. In addition to state taxes paid, New Mexico allows a taxpayer to take a credit against New Mexico state taxes due for local taxes paid as well as taxes paid to the District of Columbia. N.M. Stat. Ann. § 7-9-79(A); N.M. Stat. Ann. § 7-5-1, Art. V. No credit is allowed for taxes erroneously paid to another state. New Mexico Public Decision 17-37 (Sept. 13, 2017). BNA-SUTN NM 4.2.

Topic: Jurisdiction and Sourcing Rules Temporary Presence, In-Transit Property Tangible Personal Property First Used Out-of-state

Depends The use by an individual of personal or household effects brought into New Mexico in connection with that person establishing initial residence within New Mexico is exempt from compensating use tax, as is the use of property brought into the state by a non-resident for his own non-business use while temporarily within New Mexico. N.M. Stat. Ann. § 7-9-27. This exemption does not apply to property used in connection with engaging in business in New Mexico. The use of property for business purposes in New Mexico is not exempt from the imposition of the compensating tax. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §2.115.8. BNA-SUTN NM 4.3.1.

Topic: Jurisdiction and Sourcing Rules Temporary Presence, In-Transit Property Tangible Personal Property Temporarily Stored In-state

Depends Tangible personal property is not considered used in New Mexico for the purposes of the gross receipts and compensating use taxes when it is merely stored for subsequent sale in the ordinary course of business or for use solely outside New Mexico. N.M. Stat. Ann. §7-9-3(N). This exemption does not apply to property used in connection with engaging in business in New Mexico. The use of property for business purposes in New Mexico is not exempt from the imposition of the compensating tax. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §2.115.8. BNA-SUTN NM 4.3.2.

Topic: Jurisdiction and Sourcing Rules Temporary Presence, In-Transit Property Tangible Personal Property Purchased for Use Out-of-state

Taxable Receipts from sales of property to nonresidents of New Mexico who accept delivery of the property in New Mexico or where transfer of title or risk of loss passes to the nonresident buyer in New Mexico are not receipts from transactions in interstate commerce and are not deductible under N.M. Stat. Ann. § 7-9-55. As such, these transaction are taxable. N.M. Admin. Code tit. 3, §2.213.12. BNA-SUTN NM 4.3.3.
